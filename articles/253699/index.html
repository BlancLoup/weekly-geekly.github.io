Who came up with the first computer virus?

It began rather slowly and much earlier than it was supposed. The first viruses spread via offline - they worked with diskettes and were transferred to them between computers. Who invented the virus?

The first virus for Mac was written as a teenage joke. The first PC virus was made to combat piracy.

Elk cloner




In 1981 , Richard Skrenta was in grade 9. He was a very smart hooligan armed with an Apple II computer. Most of all he liked to mock peers about their pirated computer games. In 2000, in an interview, he said :

I joked at my peers by changing copies of pirated games so that they self-destruct after a certain number of launches. I handed out games, sat down on them, and then she suddenly stopped working and gave out some funny comment on the screen (a ninth-grader's sense of humor).


As a result, friends have ceased to allow Skrent to their floppy disks. He stopped borrowing games, everyone stopped playing his toys, etc. But he did not calm down. He began studying instructions and descriptions, trying to find a security hole in the Apple II. And he came up with a way to execute code without touching floppy disks.

When an uninfected disk was inserted into the drive of an infected computer, it infected the disk by writing a copy of the virus to the boot sector. This code was automatically executed at boot. The virus interfered with the computer a little, and on the 50th launch, instead of launching the program, would display a whole poem on the screen:

Brain




The first virus to spread on the IBM PC was the Brain virus. He also settled in the boot sector. It was written by brothers Basit and Amjad Farouk Alvi from Pakistan in 1986. They were 17 and 24 years old.

The brothers had a computer company, Brain Computer Services, and they wrote a virus to track pirated copies of their medical software. The pirate program ate RAM, slowed down the operation of the disk, and sometimes made it difficult to save data. On assurances of the brothers, she did not destroy the data. The program contained the following message:

Welcome to the Dungeon 1986 Basit & Amjad (pvt) Ltd. BRAIN COMPUTER SERVICES 730 NIZAB BLOCK ALLAMA IQBAL TOWN LAHORE-PAKISTAN PHONE: 430791,443248,280530. Beware of this VIRUS ... Contact us for vaccination ... $ # @% $ @ !!


Welcome to the dungeon ... Beware of this virus ... Contact us for treatment ...

In the title were indicated real contacts. When someone called them for help, they could identify the pirated copy. Also, the virus counted the number of copies made.
They discovered that piracy was widespread, and copies of their programs were distributed very far. Amjad says that their first call came from the United States, Miami.


Alvi brothers in 2011

This was the first of many calls from the United States. The problem turned out to be that Brain spread on other diskettes, and not just on copies of their program. There was even an epidemic of this virus at the University of Delaware in 1986, and then it appeared in many other places. No lawsuits were filed, but many wrote about it in the newspapers. The creators were even mentioned in Time Magazine in 1988.

The New York Times wrote in May 1988 : ‚ÄúThe daring computer program, which appeared on the computers of the Providence Bulletin this month, destroyed the files of one correspondent and spread through diskettes throughout the entire network of the newspaper. Computer scientists believe that this is the first case of infection of a computer system of an American newspaper with such an audacious program, which is called a computer "virus".

The Alvi brothers had to change phones and remove contacts from later versions of the virus. They stopped selling the program in 1987 . Their company has grown into a telecommunications provider and now it is the largest provider in Pakistan. It is located all at the same address.
