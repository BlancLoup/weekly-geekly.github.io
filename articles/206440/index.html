About the formulation of the problem

When I read the post about pulsed electrolysis, the idea was formed immediately. The outbreaks were characteristic yellow in color, apparently from the atomic emission of sodium contained in water. If we obtain the spectrum of this glow and measure its intensity at a wavelength of 589 nm, then there should be a dependence on the sodium content in water. Essence of the work

We get the plasma above the surface of the analyzed water using a spark from the ignition coil, decompose it into a spectrum using a diffraction grating on top of the camera lens, fill it with spectralworkbench.org for calculation, do a signal and concentration calibration, PROFIT.

Atomic emission

There is a way to measure the content of sodium and potassium in liquid objects. If you transfer the sample to steam at a temperature of a thousand to one and a half degrees Celsius, then sodium turns into a state of atomic vapor and begins to emit very intensively monochrome yellow light with a wavelength of 589 nm. The intensity of radiation depends linearly on the sodium content in the sample, that is, you can measure several solutions with a known concentration and obtain the coefficients of this linear equation:
I = a * c + b, where I is the intensity, c is the concentration.
To obtain atomic vapor, sample injection into the flame of a gas burner is usually used; the device with all the giblets takes up a small cabinet. Why not try replacing it all with a small spark generator?
