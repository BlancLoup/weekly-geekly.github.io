Robokot changes the color of "wool" depending on the environment





A group of engineers from the Chinese University of Wuhan has developed a robot that can change its color depending on the color scheme of its environment. Details of the work done are set out in the ACS Nano magazine. Unique in its kind, the chameleon robot is made in the form of a cat, whose body elements are printed on a 3D printer. Each of the elements of which the visible surface of the robot body is composed is equipped with an integrated sensor and a miniature plasmon ‚Äúdisplay‚Äù - a small glass plate with a recess with a diameter of 50 Nm. The internal gilded surface of the recesses is coated with an electrolytic gel with silver ions.

When each such ‚Äúpixel‚Äù interacts with the incident light, the thin upper layer of gold emits plasmons and becomes reddish. When voltage is applied to the electrolytic gel, silver ions are in contact with gold, which is accompanied by a visual change in hue, and the change in the magnitude of the applied voltage allows the visible color to vary. The absence or discharge of voltage to zero leads to leveling of the described effect. The sensors used in the experiment to recognize the color gamut of the background and the magnitude of the voltage required for the supply responded to three basic colors (see video) ‚Äîblue, green, and red. At the same time, the developed technology does not impose any restrictions on the creation of more complex structures with more advanced sensors that quickly and accurately respond to any changes in the background color gamut.



Detailed results of the experiment are described on the pages of the online magazine ASC Nano. A brief report is given in the edition of newscientist.com .

Already in the short term, the engineers note, the developed technology of masking can be improved and will allow you to create instantly camouflage military camouflage or means that can hide any military equipment using the same principle.

Why did you need to create a robot?





Researchers at the University of California at Berkeley have developed a robot belt, which has an amazing ability not only to carry multiple compressive loads, but also to move in such a state in tight spaces of confined space. The development has been published in details in the PNAS journal (via the link pdf-file) with a brief annotation on the university website .

The researchers were inspired by the creation of a robotic cockroach, its living types from the family Periplaneta americana. Having conducted many experiments on test benches specially created for this purpose, the scientists came to the conclusion that insects are able to withstand a load of 900 (!) Times their own weight. At the same time, which is typical, cockroaches did not lose the ability to move quickly enough even when their body was squeezed 3-4 times in comparison with its natural state, and the ability to use the legs in accordance with their anatomical features was extremely difficult.



As it turned out, effective movement in such a cramped state was possible thanks to the sensitive hairs on the legs, working in ‚Äúnormal mode‚Äù as a tactile organ. In such conditions, excessive friction arises between the legs and the floor, while between the shell and the surface pressed from above is much smaller.


CRAM and cockroach on the deformation stand

By transferring the described principle to the construction of a robotic wire, the researchers were able to create a deformable CRAM robot. The legs of a specific shape are fixed along the edges of the elastic shell. When the exoskeleton is deformed, the legs rest against the bottom surface with another part of it, thus preserving the possibility of movement.

CRAM robot is able to withstand a load of up to one kilogram, which is 20 times its own weight. The robot keeps its movement ability almost doubled - from 75 to 35 millimeters. According to the researchers, in the future, robots with similar capabilities will be in demand as ideal scouts in disaster areas, when they need to penetrate into the cracks, much smaller than the size of their corps.
