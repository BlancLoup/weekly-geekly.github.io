Previous and next web forms action

Among designers, questions about placing buttons (actions) in web forms are often discussed. A typical question from this discussion is:

Can the action that leads to the previous step of the process be placed to the right of the action that leads forward?

One side of the discussion argues that it is a natural organization: actions that lead forward are placed on the right, back and left. The other side is of the opinion that the concept of ‚ÄúPrimary‚Äù actions (those that move people forward) outweighs this consideration and that ‚ÄúSecondary‚Äù actions (which are less used) can be placed to the right of ‚ÄúPrimary‚Äù, even if ‚ÄúSecondary‚Äù lead to previous steps. The presence of highly visible primary actions in the course of the users' view (on the ‚Äúline of fire‚Äù) allows us to focus on the final actions of filling out the form.

Both sides! One of the reasons why there are so many discussions on this topic is that too often web designers (developers) create a ‚ÄúMaster‚Äù (from Windows) in which users move through a chain of steps.

Placing primary actions on the user's way (immediately under the last question of the form) makes it clear that he can and should move forward. When moving along a linear flow of information (such as placing an order in online stores), returning to the previous stage can be difficult; such scenarios can be covered by checking the entered information in the final window. In fact, research shows that very few users use the ‚ÄúBack‚Äù button in the forms.
