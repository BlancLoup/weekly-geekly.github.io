7 strategies to help you get rid of procrastination

I strongly suspect that you are pulling time so as not to undertake any important matter, even at the moment, by reading this article. It turns out some kind of paradox, but it does not matter. It is even good. Here you will learn simple strategies that you will be able to apply in the future to effectively combat procrastination.




Procrastination can occur for a variety of reasons:

  • Fear of success;
  • Fear of failure;
  • Fear of condemnation;
  • Fear of the unknown;
  • Perfectionism and excessive attention to detail;
  • Over planning;
  • Unrealistic expectations;
  • Low self-esteem;
  • Lack of goals and guidelines.

Nevertheless, these seven strategies will help you solve the problem, regardless of what lies at its root. They will push you to the goal, slightly changing your way of thinking and approach to business.

Read the details on each of the strategies below, select the one that seems right for you, and try it out.

Here is the entire list:

  1. Think within 24 hours
  2. Imagine the perfect schedule and working conditions
  3. Break the task into small fragments (and gradually make them larger)
  4. Adhere to the rule of two minutes ("The action you are trying to put into the habit, should take no more than two minutes")
  5. Use the Seinfeld method
  6. Procrastinate procrastination (delay unwanted action until tomorrow)
  7. Reward yourself as soon as possible (positive reinforcement)


Let's go in order.

1. Think within 24 hours


‚ÄúWhere your focus is there is your energy‚Äù - Tony Roobins

Over the past seven months, this approach has allowed me to dramatically increase my own productivity, get rid of many bad habits and get useful.

Keep in mind: it may take months to bring the project to the end or to fix some kind of habit, but you should not look so far into the future. Of course, it is necessary to plan, but we tend to overload ourselves with similar thoughts and eventually lose our motivation.

So if you set a goal to lose weight in two months, think only about today. Your task is to try your best to eat properly for the next 24 hours. And that's all. No need to think about what will happen next week or even tomorrow. When the day comes to an end, then it will be possible to think about the priorities for the next day.

Go to the second paragraph.

2. Imagine the perfect schedule and working conditions


‚ÄúTo attract something into your life, imagine that it is already there‚Äù - Richard Bach

Visualization has an interesting property: it stimulates us to action and can give motivation to take a step towards the goal.

What would be your ideal daily routine and working environment?

Answer the following questions:

  • When do you wake up? And further, what do you do immediately after waking up?
  • What is your main goal and what should you do in a day to get closer to it? What does your workspace look like?
  • Do you fall into a state of flow? How many hours a day would you like to spend at work?
  • What are your hobbies? Reading, dancing, fencing (I know, an unusual example)?
  • What do you do before you go to bed? Do you have any rituals?
  • And finally, what do you feel?


Just imagining yourself how your day should ideally take shape, you awaken motivation in yourself - it is a very powerful tool.

3. Break the task into small fragments.


‚ÄúGreatness is made up of little things that are done well. Day after day, training after training, assignment after assignment, day after day ‚Äù- Ray Lewis

So, you have defined a time frame for 24 hours and have drawn a picture of the perfect day. The next step is to get started.

Determine which task is most important to achieve the goal, and take a small step towards accomplishing it. Nothing significant, no sudden jumps. Take this as a habit formation - not volume, but constancy is important here.

Get started today: choose one small task for 15 minutes and try to do it as best you can. The next day, add a few more minutes and refine what you did. Keep up the good work until you reach the optimal number of working hours per day. Which is determined in its ideal order, remember?

And then just learn, adapt and act.

Repeat the procedure until you learn to deal with procrastination.

And what if we apply this feature to undesirable actions?

I decided to conduct an experiment. Choose one habit and focus on it for 24 hours. If there is a temptation to break it, I will postpone this impulse - until tomorrow.

You will achieve results, even if you test only one of the methods on your chosen goal. However, you can also combine these strategies - each one will produce a result, and in the end you will get a cumulative effect.
