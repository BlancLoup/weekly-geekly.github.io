Concepts


Concepts are such a cool thing that extends the use of templates. In short, the concept allows you to put forward the requirements for the type used in the template. For example, you can require a public constructor and destructor, support for a specific interface, and some other things. There is a thought that concepts could be quite useful - to reduce the number of errors, to avoid foolish compiler messages when working with template types, to give the user understanding of the template type about what this type does with its argument. In general, cool. Something similar is, for example, in C #.
The concepts were removed from draft C ++ 0x in July 2009 with the wording ‚Äúnot ready‚Äù. There were several reasons for this:
  1. In the proposed form it turned out too fancy. The supplement describing concepts took up one and a half hundred pages (while the rest of the standard was about 800). It was a bit hard. We tried to simplify - but it turned out even worse. Half of the people began to complain that they cut the necessary things, the second - that the rest is still too difficult.
  2. A couple of separate groups of programmers in the framework of a personal initiative tried to make the implementation of concepts in the compiler. In GCC, of ‚Äã‚Äãcourse. It turned out, for example, this and a couple more options. All of them showed a very mediocre performance both in compilation and in the work of the resulting programs. And not only on examples using concepts, but also on ordinary templates. The implementations were recognized as "proving a theoretical possibility", but not suitable for real use.
For over 2 years, different people tried to do something with all this mess. But 2 years for the entire crowd that is trying to develop a new C ++ standard is just an instant. Nothing was solved. Those who know what it is, in this place shuddered.
