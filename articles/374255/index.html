What is meditation


First, let's understand what meditation is. There are so many things around the term that the meaning is now not so simple to understand.


The term itself came from India, but to refer to it when translating, they used the Latin word meditatio - meditation. In the original, in Sanskrit, meditation was called dhyana. This word, for example, in Wikipedia, is translated as contemplation, ‚Äúseeing with the mind,‚Äù ‚Äúintuitive seeing,‚Äù meditation, concentration, thinking. Which gives us a hint that this is something related to the mind. However, for practical understanding it gives a little.


If you read a Buddhist wiki about meditation , you can see that there it is defined as a method of transforming the mind.


Moreover, this is not just one kind of frozen method, but a huge number of practical techniques that can be both rigidly fixed for everyone and individually applied depending on the mindset of the practitioner. Buddhism, which is the most densely used method of meditation and studies a lot of them, emerged around 500 BC and to our time a huge number of practitioners and scientists have continuously used and improved these methods to transform the mind in their chosen directions.


So, we will try to give a definition for the purposes of our article. Meditation is a method for working with the mind, most often (but not necessarily) consisting of thinking on a given topic and focusing the mind in some described way, which is aimed at transforming the practitioner's mind in the chosen context.


For example, we may feel greed overwhelms us and wish to regulate this state. To do this, we can begin the daily practice of specially developed meditation, usually 10 to 30 minutes a day and, after some time, this state should return to normal.
