Docker and everything is everything


Today there are few who disagree with the fact that Docker eventually became the "mainstream". Well, more precisely, the Docker containers have become. True, there is a front where the war is not over yet, although for many the winner is already obvious, and the winner is Kubernetes, which, according to use statistics, is far ahead of its main competitors: Docker Swarm and Mesos + Mesosphere.


I know people and companies that disagree with the majority‚Äôs conclusion that k8s is the future of container orchestration. But for some reason, similar tools from other manufacturers also often do not suit them. Large companies in this case choose the path of developing their own tool for orchestrating containers. Others stop at Mesos or even Nomad. But I don‚Äôt yet know anyone who would use Docker Swarm (mode).


By the way, the number "8" in the word k8s means exactly "8 letters", that is: k8s = k [8_other_books] s = kubernetes. The fashion for such reductions appeared in the bearded 1980s of the last century, and I personally do not welcome such reductions, therefore I will continue to call the tools by their full name.


The situation with HYIP around Kubernetes (which is exactly HYIP in my opinion) makes vendors worry about its support. It got to the point that even Docker announced the addition of official support for Kubernetes in the next version of Docker EE (Enterprise Edition), as well as dev versions of Docker CE (Community Edition) for Windows / macOS (yes, it‚Äôs not about the Linux version yet) .


Supporting Kubernetes inside Docker does not automatically stop the development of Swarm, on the contrary, Docker will continue to actively invest in Swarm, while gaining additional benefits from supporting the two systems at once, commented the news David Messina , head of marketing at Docker.


Whatever the reality may be, but the words of David Messina are like the truth - the Docker architecture is designed so that the orchestration layer is below the top layer of abstraction. This makes it possible in theory to easily change the logic of the orchestrator and even replace it completely with another component. In practice, for interchangeability of orchestrators (Swarm and Kubernetes, in particular), they should have a similar interface, which Docker actively sought from Docker version 1.12 (2016), when services first appeared, and later, in version 1.13, Docker Compose was introduced 3.0, whose format is very similar to the Kubernetes resource configuration description format.


As a result, we come to the fact that when using Docker services we will have a choice of at least two orchestrators: Swarm or Kubernetes. And yes, it is only in Docker EE for now. But on the other hand, do you really want to serve Kubernetes on your own?
