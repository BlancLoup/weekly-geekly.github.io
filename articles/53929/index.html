Programming doesn't suck! At least it should not be.

Translation of an article from the Enfranchised Mind blog.

Recently, I came across two texts that I just could not help but answer. The first, the most terrible, is the article on the Daily WTF ‚ÄúProgramming - sucks! Or something like that . ‚Äù The second is a post about why functional programming does not get distributed .

Having tried myself one way or another in almost all areas of the IT industry, I am convinced that there are no uninteresting non-trivial problems. For a very simple reason: any boring, trivial, mechanical work should be a machine. No seriously! We are programmers . Who, if not us, is able to ease the work with the help of computers, if we do the same for others?

Programming can be sucked only for stupid. Five lines of code repeated over and over to set user interface properties? Oh god Have you ever heard of subroutines? Abstractions? How about generating code, or maybe it‚Äôs worth writing a DSL ? Well, if you copied these five lines just a couple of times, no big deal. But if you do it again and again (sometimes editing these lines slightly), you should probably think about how to abstract from it.

Abstractions help to increase the ratio of interesting and routine work, reducing the amount of tedious code. If instead of five lines of code repeated ten thousand times, you were able to write one line, repeated the same ten thousand times, the project was reduced from fifty to ten thousand lines of code that you can write at least five, or even twenty-five times faster. Get rid of dirty work as soon as possible, and you will have more time to do some really interesting things.

I did not say a word about changing the development language, I am only talking about thinking outside the box, or at least just thinking . But learning new languages ‚Äã‚Äãmakes programming more attractive, because at least it gives you new tools and methods of abstraction. Some programming languages are actually better than others, and the more you know them, the more likely you are to know some good language. If you do not know anything better, you can not use its benefits.

My first impression of an article on the Daily WTF was that this was another manifestation of the starving child in Africa. Well, you saw the pictures. A child in some kind of damn hole of the third world, usually in Africa, but Asia or South America will go. Hungry children, tormented by disease and parasites, doomed to a short life, full of hardship and cruelty. But they are smiling happily because they know nothing but such a life. But the second post convinced me that in fact the situation is much worse. This is not just ignorance. Many programmers not only do not know, but do not want to know anything better, and seek any excuse to remain in ignorance. As if the hungry child refused the food offered.

The fact is that I have already heard every single argument from that post. You see, I witnessed the last Great Paradigm Shift when the industry moved from procedural programming to object-oriented programming. In addition, I heard echoes of the previous revolution - the transition from non-structural to procedural programming. I heard all these arguments, more precisely, excuses, from people who do not want to learn C ++ and Java.

These excuses do not climb into any gate. Watch yourself:

  • Any example shorter than a few thousand lines of code is not serious and therefore cannot be taken into account. Real programs consist of tens and hundreds of thousands of lines, and saving a couple of lines here and there does not play any role. Any example longer than a couple of dozen lines will not be read by anyone, and therefore it is also not taken into account.
  • Any example that uses advanced language features proves that the language is too complex and difficult to learn. Any example that does not use such features is rejected, because in fact they do not write that way in this language.
  • Any example that does not relate directly to the area in which a given programmer is currently engaged is rejected. 3D graphics for game developers, server applications and database access for business logic, a graphical interface for desktop application developers. ‚ÄúWhat is the use of your stories if you don‚Äôt write my code?‚Äù
  • You can not directly compare the effectiveness of different teams of developers, because everyone has different abilities. It is also impossible to compare the effectiveness of the same developers in different languages, because the abilities of a particular language are different. You can not use the same methods to solve one problem, because this way to a specific language, and in another language must be solved differently. But if you still apply different methods, then there is nothing to compare warm with soft. You do not have a finished comparison? So you have no evidence and your opinion biased.
  • There are hundreds of person-years in the compiler of my language, and it generates much faster code than your compiler, the optimizer of which was written by a hacker on the weekend. So it was and always will be.
  • A language can become popular only when shelves in bookstores are full of benefits, it is mentioned in glossy magazines that your boss reads, and thousands of programmers line up for thousands of jobs with this language required. That is, the only way to become popular is to be popular. And since your language is not popular, it will never become so.
  • Your language is difficult to learn. My language is simple because I already know it. People will not learn what they do not yet know.
I heard how each of these arguments was used against C ++ and Java in due time. If this were true, we would still program in Fortran and Cobol. In general, we do it anyway. The cobol was not by itself. The bad thing is that you had to write five lines of code to set the parameters of the interface. Worse, the programmers in Cobol did not even try to isolate abstractions, but simply copied these five lines again and again.

How to take the first step? First, learn a new language.
