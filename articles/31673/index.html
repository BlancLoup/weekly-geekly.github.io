The next generation Mac keeps surprises

The upcoming new generation of personal computers from Apple may have undergone one of the most significant changes in architecture since the transition from PowerPC processors to Intel.

In addition to switching to Intel chips in early 2006, the Cupertino-based company also refused to use its own motherboards that support the central processor in the Mac. Instead, it has relied on modified versions of Intel chipsets, created according to industry standards for a wide range of PC manufacturers engaged in the development of Intel-compatible systems.

While the existing MacBook and MacBook Pro line is unique in that it supports Mac OS X, the architecture of these laptops involves the same components of the mobile platform Intel Santa Rosa, processors and chipsets that are used in Windows-compatible systems of such competitors like Dell and HP.

But it seems that Apple, which seeks to maintain Mac sales growth at a level twice as high as the average in the industry, is again going to isolate the architecture of its personal computers with alternative technology that will give the company an advantage beyond the reach of competitors.

Familiar with these plans, people say that the upcoming Mac generation, headed by a triple notebook with a new design , will not use the Montevina chipset, announced earlier this month as part of the mobile platform Intel Centrino 2. Moreover, these same people assume that the chipset in the new The wave of the Mac will have little to do with Intel at all.

What kind of alternative Apple has chosen is still a mystery. But it is believed that the company Intel, refused to comment on this rumor, will have to provide the Mac manufacturer with a license to use their processors on alternative chipsets.

The Mac manufacturer is committed to reducing the size and weight of its laptops, while at the same time increasing battery life and providing new technologies that are not available to competitors.
