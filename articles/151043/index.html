Fifteen myths about data recovery and loss

Over the years, we at Storelab Data Recovery have collected a small collection of myths about data loss and recovery. Some of them are actively replicated on various computer and not very forums - so, hopefully, this article will be useful to those who are looking for help when the disk is broken or data is lost. So a post on how not to make a difficult situation worse.

1. Shockproof enclosures for external drives


This is a marketing move - rubberized cases do not save the hard drive from damage after a fall, for example. They, of course, look beautiful and spectacular, and give the hard disk the look of reliability, inspiring confidence in the buyer - they are buying from them. However, they do not significantly reduce the overload on the parts of the disk during impact or fall. This can save on the condition of a small height (up to about 50 cm for 2.5 inch hard drives, up to 20 cm for 3.5-inch disks) and for a disk in the off state. In the on state, the disk almost always fails, because The maximum allowable overload on a working disk is 5-7 times less than that of a disconnected one. We carried out a crash test of Samsung, Hitachi, WD, Seagate double-byte drives. Dropped on, starting from a height of 5 cm, then ten, then every 10 cm. All the disks, except Samsung, were broken after 15 cm. Samsung broke 40 cm due to rubber dampers standing between the hard and the case. All drives were external 3.5-inch.

You can especially destroy data if you turn on a disk and then drop it from a height of about one meter (see the previous paragraph). On its surface, beautiful blisters form, after the appearance of which the data will no longer be recovered.

12. Solid State Drives - Non-Killable


There are no moving parts in a solid-state drive, as in a hard disk, and there is nothing special about spinning and breaking in it. However, this does not mean that they can play football - they are not insured against collapse. In an effort to make them smaller in size and larger in volume, manufacturers sometimes forget to make them more reliable. In addition, from normal fall or drowning, these drives are protected just like any hard drive: only by your own prudence and good fortune. In addition, they are more expensive and have a limited number of rewrite cycles. In addition, SSDs released before 2009 were often slower than the HDD, especially after long use.

15. It is possible to recover data after complete rewriting by residual magnetization.


This myth comes from the past, when computers were large and hard computers were small. On hard disks up to 1MB there was a byte-by-byte record, that is, simply put, one disk cluster was equal to one byte during recording. In this case, it made sense to look for the residual magnetization and restore data on it. Now, when the data is recorded sector-by-sector, and in one sector 512 bytes, and the recording density of tens of gigabytes per square centimeter is no longer possible.
