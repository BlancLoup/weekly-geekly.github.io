The origins of social networks - the theory of six links

This morning I remembered the so-called. the theory of six links. That time came to see where she came to our world. Those who are already briefly familiar with it can immediately go to the last part of the topic, and those who are still familiar with it are invited to take a short historical journey.

In 1929, the Hungarian author Frigyes Carinti published a series of short stories ‚ÄúEverything is different‚Äù. One of these stories was called "Chain" or "Chain Links" and described in abstract terms many of the problems that will fascinate future generations of mathematicians, sociologists, and physicists in the field of network theory (and they are still fascinating to this day). In particular, Karinti believed that the modern world is "decreasing" due to the growth of "connectedness" of people. Due to technical progress in the field of communications and transport, the networks have no boundaries in terms of size and distance. Carinti believed that, despite the long distances separating people, the increase in the density of social and virtual networks makes the social distance negligible.

The result of the Carinti hypothesis was the conclusion: any two people can be connected through a maximum of five friends. He's writing
... From this discussion has grown an exciting game. One of us proposed to put the following experiment to prove that the inhabitants of the earth are much closer to each other than they were ever before. We choose any person from 1.5 billion inhabitants of the Earth ... Using no more than five people, each of whom is a personal acquaintance of the other, a person can contact any inhabitant of the planet, regardless of the social status of the selected final links ...

This idea both directly and indirectly influenced the process of the formation of social networks.

It was a history page. On the Wikipedia page you will find a lot of materials on the topic from films to links to modern attempts to reproduce the experiment.
