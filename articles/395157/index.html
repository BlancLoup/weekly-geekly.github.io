Evaluation criterion


What is my quality criterion? Immediately it should be said that I do not care about such things as white balance, natural colors, cyanosis, distortion in the corners (distortion), blackouts in the corners (vignetting) and so on and so forth, which may be quite relevant for a home video camera or action -camera Therefore, I will not shoot for the test contrasting branches of trees against the sky and a fence extending into the distance, it may be interesting, but not practical.
The most important criterion for assessing the quality of a DVR is the ability to read license plates, the most difficult is with minimum illumination (twilight, night). The secondary criterion will be a normal response to the exposure of your own headlights or other bright light sources. And only then the rest of the comfort options, such as the availability of GPS, screen, usability and the like.
Night - because it is the most difficult mode for the registrar (in St. Petersburg there is a short light day for more than half a year, so even those who ride only from work and to work go in the dark). Plus - I have recorders in the car around the clock and always write.

Some theory


Vision angle


The larger the angle, the more it hits the video, with a wide-angle lens you can see who cuts you from the side, but the smaller the distance from which the numbers are visible in front. For a DVR, the optimum angle is about 110-120 degrees horizontally.

We have only 1920-2500 pixels wide. The wider the angle, the smaller the objects in the frame.

Why we are not interested in the diagonal angle of view?
Here are two shots taken by different lenses from the same point:
Due to lens distortion (distortion), the first frame will have a much larger diagonal angle, although horizontally it is the same for both.


At the same time, manufacturers like to specify a diagonal angle (and more, more), and do not like to indicate the real horizontal. And in vain, because of this, a lot of confusion in the heads of buyers.
NB It is not clear why the Chinese have not yet made recorders with a frame of format not 16x9, but, say 25x5, i.e. very wide and long. Anyway, in most cases, the bottom of the frame is occupied by the hood, and the top is the sky.


NB when watching a video shot from the recorder with a viewing angle of 70-80 degrees, it seems subjective that the speed of the machine is greater than it actually is, and with a angle of 120 degrees - less.

Results of night tests:


- Big FPS - does not give any bonuses.
- Large resolution - does not give any bonuses.
- The use of WDR mode really significantly increases the level of reading rooms in low light.
- WDR in Vico / Dome works better than HDR in Inspector, however, even the latter slightly improves the detail of the night mode.
- GoPro Hero4 did not cope with night modes, in many cases showing results worse than the old Vico, LowLight mode made the picture a little lighter and more distinguishable, but added no details.

Total comparison results


- Specialized devices (DVRs) are not in vain, they are markedly different from multi-purpose devices (action cameras, video cameras).
- Over the past 5 years, the quality of video recorders has changed significantly. Let it not be embarrassing that when viewed on YouTube everything seems to be about the same, in small things (reading numbers) hardware and software makes visible what was unattainable five years ago.
- Noticeable quality jump in the transition from Ambarella A7 to Ambarella A12.
- Modern top recorders are able to show machine numbers:
a) stop, in the same lane - at a distance of about 10-12 meters in the afternoon, and up to 6-8 meters without lighting.
b) movement, the next row - when driving with a speed difference of about 60-70 km / h during the day, and up to 15-20 km / h at night.
After the tests, it is safe to say that to choose a good recorder, it is important that it uses the latest generation processor, for Ambarella it is A12 or A7. Having SuperHD resolution is a good factor. The rest - choose to your own taste and manner of use, well, read the tests.

Carefully look at the work of WDR / HDR for a specific device. I reviewed a bunch of videos, many registrars, despite the announcement of support for wide dynamic range modes, do not have any advantages to reality at night. We have to admit that WDR has become a marketing symbol, which is molded almost everywhere.

In terms of creativity, I have a slight curvature, so I had to jot down a stack of video processing scripts in order to operate only with numbers and exclude hand curves from the process of preparing videos and graphics. I share the accumulated knowledge in a brief format of examples:
Hidden text
- First of all, in order to avoid information loss due to massive transcoding, I would recommend to transfer all available formats to loosless h.265 (or any other loseless raw format that you are comfortable with)
trac.ffmpeg.org/wiki/Encode/H.264
Stock up on space, every minute FullHD videos will occupy ~ 1.3 Gb.

- Glue a video consisting of several pieces into one file:
echo -e "file './180318AA.mp4'\nfile './180618AA.mp4'" | ffmpeg -f concat -safe 0 -i - -c copy ./dome_full.mp4

- Cut 60 seconds of video from the second minute of 21 seconds (the order of the keys matters):
ffmpeg -i ./AMBA0003.mp4 -ss 02:21 -t 60 -c copy ./carcamq2_1_1.mp4

- Rotate the frame by 2.5 degrees counterclockwise (used only for review video, because it introduces a distortion in the picture):
ffmpeg -i ./contour_2_1.mp4 -vf "rotate=-2.5*PI/180:ow=iw:oh=ih" -acodec copy ./contour_2_1_rotated.mp4

- From the video we save all the frames, from the frames we cut out only a horizontal strip, we glue the strips one under the other into a large picture: 
 #convet video to png pictures (15 png per second) ffmpeg -i ./carcamq2_1_1.mp4 -r 15 ./picture_%04d.png # crop area=1920x100, started from x=0, y=550 for filename in ./picture_*.png ; do convert ${filename} -crop 1920x100+0+550 +repage ${filename} ; done montage ./picture_*.png -border 1 -geometry +0+0 -tile 1x ./carcamq2_1_1-full.png rm ./picture_*.png


- Cut a horizontal strip from the video, if the width of the piece is less than 1920 - add a black background on the sides: 
 # video crop=width:heigth:pos_x:pos_y ffmpeg -i gopro_2_1.mp4 -filter_complex "[0:v]crop=1600:360:iw/3:ih/3 , pad=width=1920:height=ih:x=(1920-iw)/2:y=0:color=black" ./gopro_2_1_crop.mp4


- Glue the video of three strips into one full-frame: 
 ffmpeg -i gopro_2_1_crop.mp4 -i gopro_2_2_crop.mp4 -i gopro_2_3_crop.mp4 \ -qscale 0 -filter_complex \ "pad=1920:1080[tmp1]; \ [tmp1][0:v]overlay=0:0[tmp2]; \ [tmp2][1:v]overlay=300:360[tmp3]; \ [tmp3][2:v]overlay=500:720[out]" -map "[out]" gopro_all.mp4


- Overlay transparent png over the video:
ffmpeg -i test1_day.mp4 -i test1_overlay.png -qscale 0 -filter_complex "[0:v][1:v]overlay=0:0[out]" -map "[out]" test1_day_overlay.mp4

- create a 5-second video insert from the finished image
ffmpeg -framerate 1/5 -i test1_day_overview.png -c:v libx264 -r 30 test1_day_overview.mp4

I can also recommend to immediately create a database with a list of video clips and their characteristics. For example, I used a regular .csv file that reads easily in bash, where the paths, resolutions of each video, the center point (horizon line and the visual center of the frame), the shooting mode, the time of the desired event, etc. were indicated.
