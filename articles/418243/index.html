Johann Kepler expounded his ideas about giant stars in a book he wrote in 1606 and called " De Stella Nova ", or "On a New Star." The book told about a new star, just appeared for a while out of nowhere in the sky in the year 1604. According to Kepler, the new star eclipsed everyone else, even Sirius, the brightest of all the stars, regularly appearing in the night sky. In the book, Kepler reflected on the size of the new star, and concluded that its girth is much larger than the size of the orbit of Saturn (the most distant planet known at that time). Sirius should have been about the same huge, and even the smallest stars, in his opinion, should have been greater than the orbit of the Earth.

His stars were generally the size of a universe. The former head of Kepler, Tycho Brahe , proposed a theory of the Universe, borrowed from Copernicus, according to which the Earth was stationary in the center of the Universe. Shortly before his death in 1601, Brahe personified the "great science" of his days - he had a huge observatory, the best tools, many wonderful assistants (such as Kepler), his own publishing, and a lot of money. Why did Kepler declare that the size of the stars is comparable to the universe? Because the data talked about it - at least if his heliocentric theory was correct. According to this theory, the Earth moved around the Sun in a circle, making a revolution in a year. Therefore, if at one time of the year she moved towards a certain star, then after six months she moved from this star. It was possible to expect that some stars will burn brighter in the spring when the Earth approached them, and then become dimmer in the autumn. This effect is called parallax. But nobody watched any parallax. Copernicus explained it this way: the orbit of the Earth will seem like a tiny dot compared to the distances to the stars. The Earth's orbit was negligible for the stars, and the motion of the Earth could be neglected.
