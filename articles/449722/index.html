Dynamics algorithm


It is difficult for us to see it through this vortex of equations, but all of them are its integral part. We derived enough equations in order to obtain the magnificent dynamics of a two-dimensional world with arbitrarily given forces and torques moving and rotating our objects. How to use these equations? Below is the basic algorithm:

  1. Find the magnitude of the center of mass and the moment of inertia at the center of mass.
  2. Set the initial coordinates of the body, its orientation in space, its linear and angular velocity.
  3. Take into account all the forces acting on the body and the points of their application.
  4. Find the resultant of all forces and divide it by body mass in order to find the linear acceleration of the center of mass (Equation 5).
  5. For each force, construct a scalar product with a perpendicular between the vector directed from the center of mass to the point of application of force and the vector of applied force, add this value to the total moment of force in the center of mass equation (Equation 11).
  6. Find the quotient for the total moment of force and moment of inertia at the center of mass to find the angular acceleration (Equation 14).
  7. Numerically integrate linear acceleration and angular acceleration to update coordinates, linear velocity, orientation in space, and angular velocity (see the last article).
  8. Draw an object in the resulting coordinate, and go to Step 3.

In the algorithm above there are only two steps that I have not explained. First, how to calculate the moment of inertia in Step 1 for a solid object? Secondly, how to solve the problem with the forces from Step 3? The answer to the first question can be found in a simple code example, which I will leave in the appendix at the end of this article (you will integrate the object by its area). Many books on dynamics contain the calculated moment of inertia for frequently encountered forms of objects in the application at the very end, so you do not have to display them yourself every time.

The answer to the question of how to calculate the forces from Step 3 depends on the application, but I will give some general recommendations. First, such forces as gravity, always directed in one direction (down, in the case of gravity), do not create a moment of force, since they are pulling all points at the same time in one direction, although we We apply these forces directly to the center of mass. Forces, similar to the force of elasticity, are applied to a certain point of the object, they will create a moment of force, therefore we consider them in the general case. As we saw in the first article, the friction force is the same force directed in the direction opposite to the speed of the body.

You can make a simple physical model that demonstrates frictional force and simply apply force to the center of mass, or you can choose which parts of the object friction forces will be applied to and can do this, which can create a moment of force acting on the object. The forces that bodies experience in collisions are a little more difficult, and we will get to know them in the next article. Forces, like a rocket engine, should be considered as forces with an application point (in this case, if one of the engines fails, you will start spinning around its axis until you adjust the steering wheel in order to balance the moment of force!). If you want something resembling gravitational rays from a UFO, then this force must be calculated as the force of gravity and not create a moment of force, or it must be applied to a certain point of the object,and will it rotate around that point while it rises up? The choice is yours. The key point is not to be afraid to experiment with different forces, calculated in different ways, because right now you have a real two-dimensional graphics simulator, try different types of forces!
