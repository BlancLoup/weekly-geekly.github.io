Drought affected trees emit ultrasound

April 16, 2013.
Alexandre Ponomarenko of the University of Grenoble (France) claims that trees under conditions of high stress caused by drought make sounds that can be registered and used as signs of a water deficit dangerous to the plant.

Why is this happening? In a tree trunk, water and a number of substances rise through the network of xylem vessels from the roots to the tops. Since the trees are high, in order for the delivery of water to work, the pressure in the vessels can be many times greater than the atmospheric pressure. But, as in the cocktail tube, when the water in the vessel ends and it is no longer enough to fill the entire container, the formation of bubbles may begin. As a result, when there is a lack of moisture in the xylem, cavitation processes are started, which the tree is almost unable to resist. And if their scale is too large, the plant will be injured or even die, just like a person in the case of a gas embolism, when air bubbles in the circulatory system cause serious damage to the vessels.

To identify cavitation in the tissues of the tree, scientists under the leadership of Mr. Ponomarenko used a piece of pine trunk, placed in a gel, which was designed to simulate the environment inside a living plant. Then water was gradually removed from the gel, imitating drought conditions. At the same time, the sample was filmed with a high-speed camera and the sounds coming from it were recorded on the microphone.

And so it was possible to find out: the occurrence of bubbles recorded by the camera, and the sounds of cavitation nature coincide in time, and the latter are responsible for 50% of the sounds in the ultrasonic range from the plant. The remaining half was due to extraneous processes, such as the entry of air bubbles into cells adjacent to xylem vessels.

Scientists believe that microphones tuned to such sounds are quite capable of helping to detect drought symptoms in a tree that is still looking healthy, including biocenoses, where such plants are on the verge of extinction due to global droughts that have become more frequent in recent decades. warming
