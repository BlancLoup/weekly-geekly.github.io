How does the reform of export control in the USA affect foreign purchases?



Over the past two years, online shoppers have encountered an unforeseen problem, namely the fact that some items previously available for purchase have suddenly appeared on the list of prohibited goods. For example, clothes in camouflage colors or individual fishing gear. The network here and there were debates about the reasons for such a change, but the truth, as always, was somewhere nearby and turned out to be quite banal - making changes to the legislation.

A few years ago, the US authorities launched a three-phase export control reform , which aims to streamline all existing norms and create a single automated mechanism to control the export of goods abroad, which will allow the authorities to conduct regular inspections and keep abreast of the export market. In August 2015, the reform entered into the second phase, and the federal authorities urge exporters to also bring their procedures in line with the new requirements.

What does this mean?

90% of exported goods do not require licensing and are not subject to additional regulation. Only 10% of export turnover falls under the Commerce Control List ( CCL ), and such products will be assigned to ECCN ( Export Control Classification Number ). This number indicates the characteristics of the product, which make it possible to use it for military purposes, in nuclear energy, telecommunications and several other fundamentally important sectors. Each exporter is obliged to check the processed goods for compliance with the CCL and set the classification number (ECCN), if it has been assigned to the goods by the manufacturer.

We now return to products that do not have an ECCN (collective number EAR99 ) and are not subject to the export control list. Does this mean that if the goods are not regulated, they can be exported without thinking? Not really.
Before taking out any investment outside the United States, the exporter must ensure that the shipment does not violate existing embargoes or sanctions against the country of destination, that the recipient is not on the black list of persons denied export privileges, and finally that he does not have doubt in the possible misuse of the goods.
If countries with an embargo can be counted on the fingers of one hand, at least a dozen government agencies share the lists of banned persons.
For each of the lists, you must verify the recipient data, manually or through Denied Party Screening systems.



The same goal is followed by the rule ‚Äú Know your customer ‚Äù, which encourages exporters to confirm customer data, to be sure - the final recipient is the person whose data are specified in the order, and this is a real physical or legal person.

Do not forget about the rules of the International Air Transport Association ( IATA ) and the provisions of the Universal Postal Union ( UPU ). Forbidden investment in mailings are, among others, any drugs and medical equipment (including syringes, surgical needles, etc.), liquids over 1 liter (cumulative per package), combustible substances (eg perfume), any aerosols or merchandise under pressure (eg. airbags), etc.

Failure to comply with export control requirements, either intentionally or unknowingly, entails serious consequences for both the exporter and the recipient.
