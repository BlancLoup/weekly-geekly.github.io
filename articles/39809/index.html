10 reasons why you should not quit your job and become a freelancer

Nowadays, more and more people are leaving the company and settling in home offices. Quite often they ask me a question: is it worth it to quit and start earning a living in freelancer? An unequivocal answer, of course, will not give anyone. Someone is able to independently communicate with customers, update the task list and not break the deadlines. Someone, on the contrary, can work productively only under conditions of control by the management. Let's try to find the benefits of working in the office.

    I am familiar with many talented people who show excellent professional results.     Working in a team, you have a great opportunity to improve at the expense of colleagues. Surely in your company there are people with skills that you do not have. Ask questions, get answers, grow professionally. And all this is completely free.
  8. Fixed work schedule
    Working in an office of eight hours five days a week is not a bad idea. A fixed schedule allows you to plan your free time. If your work day usually ends at 7:00 pm, you can shamelessly arrange a meeting with friends next week on Thursday at 7:30 pm.
    Still want to gain independence and work independently? Get ready for the fact that the friendly crowd will have to be postponed, and the premiere sequel about Agent 007 will be abandoned in favor of working on an urgent project.
  9. Social life
    Having decided to gain independence from the employer, get ready for the fact that one day email and icq will replace your live communication. In psychology, it is commonly called social deprivation, which, by the way, can lead to serious psychological deformations of the individual. Talking about weather with a colleague in a smoking room only at first glance may seem to be a useless exercise. I bet - after some time you will start to miss this useless chatter.
  10. Your professional reputation is protected by the company.
    Imagine that the agency you work for has lost a client. Even worse, if the refusal to cooperate occurred through your own fault (failure to meet deadlines or poor quality results). Not a very pleasant situation, right? But even from it you can extract a positive experience - the client refused to work with the company, but not with you. It protects your professional reputation at the expense of the company. The worst thing that could threaten - the dismissal. But you yourself were going to do it?
