Patent for "Secret Communication System"


Hedy Lamarr, in 1942, under his real name Kiesler, along with his friend, avant-garde composer George Anteil , received a patent for the ‚ÄúSecret Communication System‚Äù, which included the transmission of spurious channels at different frequencies.

If you report the target coordinates of the controlled torpedo remotely only at one control frequency, then you are firing and the enemy easily intercepts the signal, moreover, it can drown it or redirect the torpedo.

Lamar proposed synchronously, in accordance with a specific algorithm, changing the carrier frequency of the rocket transmitter and receiver. So the enemy will not be able to "wedge" into such a communication channel. Lamar proposed to create guided torpedoes.



During the game with Anteyl four hands on the piano, Lamarr recalled how in conversations with her ex-husband the Germans complained that they could not direct torpedoes at the target using a radio signal, since the enemy‚Äôs ships jammed signals with interference on the same frequency. Hedy suggested that you can send part of the signal at one frequency, and then switch to another to transmit the next part of the signal. When matching the transmitter and receiver in terms of frequency hopping, something like a four-hand game would result, and the signal could become resistant to jamming interference. Lamarr also suggested that the mechanical matching of the transmitter with the receiver could occur through a piece similar to a roller of a mechanical piano. A roller with pins and a chronometer drive looked compact enough to fit in the hull of a naval torpedo. In addition, according to Lamarr, the system could use a set of 88 radio frequencies - according to the number of piano keys.


Together with Anteyl, they developed the following algorithm: it is necessary to use a random code on the transmitter, it will change the transmission channel, then it will be possible to synchronize such frequency transitions on the receiver.


The change of communication channels is a guarantee of secure information transfer. To ensure synchronism of frequency change, Lamar and Antheil used ribbons with holes, such as paper tape in a mechanical piano.

At that time, pseudo-random codes were used to encrypt information that was transmitted over permanent (unchanging) open communication channels. Hedy Lamar and George Antheil took a step forward: they began to use the secret key to quickly change the channels of information transfer.

The test of 12 mechanical pianos that took part in the production of ‚ÄúThe Mechanical Ballet‚Äù by George Anteil, confirmed the performance of the system. In 1940, the idea was presented to the National Council of Inventors of America. Its chairman, Charles Kettering, who led the research department of General Motors, advised the authors to submit an application for an invention.

Lamar and Anteil already in August 1942 received a patent number 2292387 "Secret Communication System" (Secret Communication System). The patent includes a description of secret communication systems, including the transfer of spurious channels at different frequencies. This development has become the basis of Spread Spectrum - spread spectrum communications, today it is used everywhere: in mobile phones, GPS and WiFi. United States Navstar Global Positioning System (GPS) is the largest system in the world that continuously transmits spread spectrum signals. Thus, the development of Hedy Lamar is one of the most significant technical works of the 20th century, moreover, the basis of the modern US military power. All the advanced technologies of the present are indebted to Hedy Lamar.
