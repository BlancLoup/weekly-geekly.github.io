The main one is about prices .

I will not discover America by talking about pricing principles. I understand that I will disappoint many, but there's nothing you can do. The Russian market (as well as the CIS countries) is geographically within the zone controlled by Apple Europe. As you certainly know, the region of ‚Äúbig Europe‚Äù (besides Europe itself and the countries of the former USSR there includes Africa, the Middle East and, more recently, India) is second in volume compared to America (albeit significantly less). Europe has its own production base (plant in Cork, Ireland) and its own logistics. And, of course, a different tax regime than in America.

Our prices are comparable to European. This means - are equal, or slightly different in one direction or another. To compare correctly, subtract VAT from Russian prices. At the same time, price fluctuations are associated with changes in the rate. This can be illustrated with an example: the principles of pricing in the eurozone, the United Kingdom, Switzerland and, say, Norway, are the same. But the exchange rate changes, and often strongly. And prices start to vary.

How is the price formed in Russia? Very simple: the price of entry, VAT, distributor mark-up, mark-up reseller or retailer (retail network). Distributor margin is within the average Russian distribution margin. The same can be said about resellers. The retailers' margin on Apple products is slightly lower than their average. What I see ways to reduce prices for Russia?

- The reduction by Apple itself of the difference between the American and European prices;
- the convergence of the dollar and the euro;
- reduction of VAT in Russia or replacement with sales tax, as in the USA or Canada;
- reduction of retailers' margin;
- reducing the distributor's margin.

Reaching the last three points is possible with:
- reduction (sharp, to the level of Europe) rental prices for retail;
- reduction (sharp, to the level of Europe) of the cost of loans;
- increase in the purchasing power of the population and, accordingly, demand;
- the resumption and development (and cheapness) of consumer lending;
- increase to the level of Europe of the demand for high-quality technology, plus the penetration and accessibility of the Internet, plus an effective fight against piracy and gray imports.

Is the appearance of the second (third, fourth) distributor a means of lowering prices? Yes, but only in terms of reducing the distribution margin, and is within a couple of percent.

Is dumping online stores and gray dealers / retailers a means of lowering prices? At first glance, yes. But this is not so simple. This destroys the channel, reduces the number of points of sales, leads to a shortage, and, as a result, to an increase in prices. Why destroy the channel - is it bad? ‚ÄúGray‚Äù do not pay VAT, and the margin due to this earn more than official dealers. Money is not spent on support, on training sellers. Official dealers (and especially retail!), Except for the largest ones, having stopped earning, will curtail Apple sales and will trade in something else. And "gray", having sold the next batch, quietly dissolve into the air. And then, you want to buy from such, as in the electronic markets, gloomy tongue-tied guys?
