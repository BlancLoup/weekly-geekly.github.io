Sun 24x7: Calculation EROEI

Over the past 10 years, solar energy has rapidly shifted from ‚Äútoys‚Äù to the most serious projects, and the continuation of the curve of this take-off promises total domination of this type of generation in the future. Or not? In the attempts to predict, a lot of copies are broken here and there are two main complaints: the sun does not shine through the clouds at night (ie, the source is volatile) and the high energy intensity of solar cell production, and this energy does not return during the latter. ( EROEI <1)

Technically, the first problem with variability is solved - you just need to build more solar panels and a battery of sufficient capacity. However, this approach clearly exacerbates the problem with EROEI and with the cost of electricity. You can see the cost in Lazard reviews, but I did not see any attempts to calculate EROEI for a solar power station with a battery. Therefore, I decided to calculate it myself, and got a rather unexpected result, which is at the end.

To evaluate, let's calculate a power station with a lithium-ion battery located in Yuma, Arizona, USA. Why in Arizona? This is a very good place for solar power plants (one of the best in the world) and there is a lot of information on it. If here EROEI turns out to be less than one, then this will mean big problems for the sun as the basic source of electricity (for today). If EROEI is higher, then, taking into account the analysis we are going to make, it will be possible to easily apply the calculation to any place in the world.


In Yuma, by the way, there is a rather large Agua Caliente Solar Project SES with a capacity of 250 megawatts, but not 24x7. The solar cells of this station are made using thin-film technology from CdTe semiconductor, which differs from silicon by much better energy costs per kilowatt of battery power, but loses in cost.

The lithium-ionic is chosen because of the obvious universality of such a solution: if the hydroaccumulator requires a suitable landscape, then electrochemical can be put practically anywhere. In fact, lithium-ion batteries actually have a couple of advantages: the ability to play on peak demand (since the inverters of this system can almost instantly switch from charging to discharging) and the prospects for cheapness (over the past 10 years, the price is 1 kilowatt * lithium-ion cell hours dropped from $ 1,000 to $ 130).

So, let's say we need a power plant that produces 300 MW of 365 days a year, 24 hours a day , which corresponds to a production of 7200 MWh every day and 2.6 TWh * h e / e per year - about 35% of a gigawatt NPP unit. We will place our SES ‚Äú24x7‚Äù in the town of Yuma, Arizona with coordinates of 32.69265 ¬∞ north latitude and 114.62769 ¬∞ west longitude.

Exactly from this point (as the TZ ended and the implementation began) difficulties begin: the fact is that the station can be optimized by EROEI is pretty cool, for example, if you ask not a one-day battery, but a two-day battery, which in turn changes the optimum slope of the batteries, etc. P. etc. In order to find the best of this, and not random, it is necessary to make a normal engineering study at this stage. Let's start the calculation with the simplest - ‚Äúenergy income‚Äù of our power station. As we shall see, its electrochemical battery will be quite large and operate mainly with a discharge depth of less than 50%, which ensures a lifetime (for LiFePo) no worse than 10,000 cycles before degradation of 20% of capacity. 10k cycles is 27 years old, let's restrict ourselves to 25 years before the station is completely updated, and the discarded balance will compensate us for the unreadable degradation of panels and batteries.

So, for 25 years, the station should supply 65.7 TWh according to the TOR - this is our numerator in the calculation of EROEI. But how much will a joule cost to build such a station? Let's first see the necessary set of equipment.

To determine, in the most rough form, how much we need a SAT and a battery, I will use the NREL Pwatts calculator calculator. It is based on the table of sun insolation values ‚Äã‚Äãfor our point, taken from the ‚Äú standard meteorological year ‚Äù - a database of meteorological parameters for the United States with harmonized values. Using this calculator, you can get the hourly values ‚Äã‚Äãof power generation, taking into account the angles of incidence of the sun, ambient light, panel temperature and conversion losses, which was done as a basis for further calculations.


The ‚ÄúStandard Meteorological Year‚Äù is a very powerful database, with measurements of such subtleties as the solar direct (yellow curve on the graph) and indirect (blue) illumination, which allows you to accurately estimate the output of the simulated SB on cloudy days.

Now, the data obtained for a single-kilowatt SES need to be somehow optimized. You can change the ratio between the volume of solar cells and batteries (the more solar cells, the less we need to store energy in order to survive the dark days without turning off) and also the angle of installation of solar cells.

For our solar power station, the defining moments of EROEI will be cloudy winter days, for example, December 27-28, in the standard meteorological year - for these two days, the station's power factor will amount to a catastrophic 3.4% and fully determines its oversize, which will result in 95% of the rest days

In principle, it would be more correct to take and change the TOR for a more optimal one - for example, ‚Äú300 megawatts 90% of the time of year‚Äù, then the station could be several times smaller, however, we will consider this option next time, but for now - hardcore.

So, the installation angle of solar panels needs to be optimized not for maximum energy performance during the year, but for maximum performance during a couple of the worst periods - it turns out 41 degrees, and not the most optimal 32 (the difference, however, is only 5% for annual output).

The ratio of the volume of the battery and solar panels is calculated a little more difficult - as the optimum energy. Taking into account the fact that 1 electric kilowatt of a solar power station costs ~ 14 GJ ( 2016 study ), and one electric kilowatt * hour of lithium-ion batteries - about 1.6 GJ ( 2012 study ).

Hence the optimization rule - we increase the battery until we reach a situation where an increase of 8.75 kWh no longer leads to a drop in the power of solar panels by at least 1 kilowatt.
