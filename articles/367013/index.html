Study: leaving social networks makes people happier



If you feel unhappy - perhaps you should delete your accounts on social networks. This is the conclusion sociologists have come from the Danish Institute for the Study of Happiness (and there is one there). The experiment on the rejection of the social network Facebook was attended by 1095 people aged from 16 to 76 years.

Randomly selected half of the people studied were asked to completely stop using Facebook for a week. The second half of the participants became a control group. At the same time, on average, 94% of the participants in the experiment visited the social network every day, 86% of the participants very often watched the news line, and 78% hung on the network for at least half an hour a day.

Sociologists have prepared a questionnaire designed to assess the subjective quality of life of people, and conducted surveys, both before the experiment and at its end. It was found that people who refused to use the social network found themselves more satisfied with life.

Also, surveys about people's moods on the last day of the experiment showed that participants who were free from viewing news felt happier, less worried, less irritated, and more enjoying life. In addition, oddly enough, the polls revealed that the refusal to constantly view social networks increases the social activity of people in the real world and satisfaction with their social life.

It was found that after refusing to use Facebook, people have increased ability to concentrate and decrease the feeling that they have wasted time, which they could use to their best advantage.



Based on the study, sociologists made several conclusions. Social networks are a constant source of news. But this news, in essence, is an edited version of other people's life, and it distorts our perception of reality. Instead of focusing on their needs, people concentrate on what others have.
