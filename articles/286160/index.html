Homomorphic encryption




One of the problems of trampling cloud technologies in place is the lack of assurance of data integrity. Current encryption technologies leave much to be desired, and force us to either completely trust the suppliers of the "clouds" or encrypt the data before moving them to the cloud. The second option, as it is more preferable and all that (the fact that you are paranoid does not yet prove that you are not really being watched), is also more inconvenient. And here homomorphic encryption can come into play. Homomorphic encryption is a form of encryption that allows you to perform certain mathematical operations with ciphertext and get an encrypted result that corresponds to the result of operations performed with plain text. The concept for more than 30 years has been just a concept. Homomorphic encryption would allow combining different services into one, without providing data for each service separately. Over the past few years, some steps have been taken that may soon lead to the creation of fully homomorphic encryption systems.

Foggy calculations



If cloud computing can be compared to fluffy white clouds high in the sky, the computational fog surrounds us with a myriad of its minor constituents. Here, fog is considered as a set of any network-connected systems, ranging from PCs to devices from the IoT world. The main lack of cloud computing is the last mile, slow service delivery to a user from a specific data center without delays. Instead, this principle comes to providing it with systems that combine the speed of local systems and the power of cloud systems.

Wireless communication


Wireless technology is currently the hottest. The avalanche-like growth in the number of wireless devices, already in major cities around the world, eats away the bandwidth of networks. To date, the bandwidth of the frequency spectrum has been limited by the physical nature of the spectrum. All technologies that use spectrum face these physical limitations. The question arises: how to connect an unlimited number of devices using limited spectrum bands? This is a major spectrum related issue. It has become one of the main directions of development of communication technologies and will be of great importance in the coming decades.
