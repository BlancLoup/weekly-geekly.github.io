Why so cheap?


The cost of any electronic product consists of four parts: the electronic components themselves, the work of assembling them into a device (assembly), the cost of output testing and the mark-up (profit). It is clear that to reduce the price you can ‚Äúbeat‚Äù on one of these parts or on all at once; again, it is clear that profits should be required. Therefore, simple logic suggests: the cheaper an electronic device, the lower the quality of electronic components, their assembly and testing, we end up with.

How much can you save on assembling a flash drive? In principle, a few cents: the amount of solder is minimal there, a piece of PCB is also small, the tracks are etched only from two sides, intermediate layers are not made. Therefore, such a ‚Äúsavings‚Äù makes sense only when mass production of tens of thousands of devices, so that the ratio ‚Äúprice / quality‚Äù does not fail the ratio ‚Äúrefusals / reputation‚Äù. True, I had to see flash drives, divorced on polystyrene slightly thicker than office paper, but this is a rare exception: they usually do not save on this.

Let's go further. What is a flash drive testing? Thrust - decided. Formatted - formatted. Recorded data - recorded, erased - erased. For a change, we recorded some pattern in all sectors, controlled it (verification) - everything was recorded. Measured the speed of operations - in the normal range. That's probably all. How can we save money if, in theory, all operations can be performed using a small tester program that an average programmer can write for a couple of bottles of beer? Of course, you can test nothing at all - time will be saved - but, as practice shows, it is better to catch an assembly marriage before it was sold, than to butt with a disgruntled customer, catch negative reviews about your product and all that jazz.

In general, whatever one may say, it turns out that the maximum savings can be made only on the use of very inexpensive electronic components. And what is the main component of a flash drive? Of course, the NAND chip.

And what's the hoax?


Let's go. The main methods to cheat with two flash drives.

1) Non-conforming NAND-memory . Manufacturers of these flash drives are buying from the factories or near-landfill rejecting chips. These are not necessarily 16 GB chips - any capacity will fit. There may be two by 8, or one by 64, but with a bunch of defective pages, etc. Such chips are not sold by the piece, but by weight, and the price is low (about half a dollar per chip after rejection of absolutely frank junk, which you just can't use). Everything goes into the business - cutting large chips, combining several small chips inside a single device, combining large chips (I personally observed two NAND chips of 64 GB each, which at the output gave ... 8 GB of total capacity), etc. Uncover fantasy and invent options.

2) Conditioned NAND-memory , but significantly less than the declared capacity . Such NAND chips (say, 1 GB) even from ‚Äúgood‚Äù manufacturers can be bought at a price already lower than a dollar per piece; and if you take a substandard, then the input of one such chip will be in the region of a few cents. It is clear that the installation of such a chip in the device, if the buyer finds that he was deceived, will not lead to anything good for the brand, so the ‚Äúmanufacturer‚Äù is going to be a trick. The capacity of the device is increased to the desired (in our case, it is 16 GB) through a passport, that is, at the ‚Äúget ID‚Äù command, the device will give as many gigabytes as it is painted on its case. In order for the fraud to be revealed not immediately, the recording on such a device or looped (gigabyte ran out - drove to write again), or emulated (gigabyte ran out - we pretend to write, but do not write anything). In my practice, there have been enough of such and such devices.

Of course, there are other methods to cheat. For example, you can never put a NAND chip inside the case of a flash drive - the flash drive will ‚Äúwork‚Äù thanks to the wired firmware, but you cannot format or write data to such a flash drive.

For the sake of justice, it should be said that I met such a brazen deception only a couple of times during the whole period of my more than 25 years of practice, however, it should not be excluded as a potential opportunity.
