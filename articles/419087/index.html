Understanding someone else's consciousness is a myth

Even experts cannot predict outbreaks of violence or suicide. We probably just deceive ourselves, believing that we can look into the minds of other people.

All this is based on the assumption that we can understand with a sufficiently high degree of reliability what is happening in the head of another person. Psychologists call this assumption a model of the human psyche (MUF; also, an understanding of another's consciousness, the theory of intentions, the theory of consciousness, the theory of mind ...). It is believed that this opportunity to perceive the fact that other people have their own, separate consciousness, which contains potentially different opinions and beliefs, intentions and desires, is one of the exceptional cognitive abilities that distinguish us from other beings.

It is not surprising that we consider ourselves able to understand the mental state of other people and to predict their behavior. We love to analyze personality by nature, impose restrictions on behavior, admire and hate. With outstretched hands, we take on like-minded people and fight with the ‚Äúwhite crows‚Äù. Reading the mind is a social glue that permeates virtually all of our daily interpersonal communication. Trying to understand whether a potential buyer of a pistol is prone to violence, a patient of a psychiatric clinic suicide, candidate presidents to truthful statements, we devote ourselves to the mercy of our judgments about other people.

The fate of democracy depends on our ability to perceive and accept different points of view - however, the almost universal absence of reasonable public debate suggests that we rarely do it. We blame people who have a different point of view for personal flaws, hidden prejudice, lack of education, cultural brainwashing and many other flaws of logic from the category "if they only knew." But there is one more simple and frightening opportunity. What if we really are just not able to thoroughly understand what others have in mind?

To begin with, let's assume the impossible - that we can go beyond our consciousness and see how the MUF can work. A psychologist presents a child of two dolls - Sally, who has a basket, and Ann, who has a box. Sally puts a ball in her basket, and then leaves the room. Until Sally is back, Ann takes the ball from the basket and hides it in her box. Sally returns to the room. After that, the child is asked a question: where will Sally search for the ball? By about four years of age, most children begin to realize that Sally will look for him in her basket (where she left him), and not in Ann's box. This universal ability of children to undergo various versions of such a test, in the absence of brain development abnormalities such as autism, is often used by cognitive scholars as irrefutable evidence that a person is able to recognize the thoughts of another person.

To understand this even deeper, in recent years, neuroscientists have come up with many alluring theories about how our brain can do this. The first promising mechanism was described in 1992, when Italian neurobiologist Giacomo Rizzolatti and his colleagues discovered that when rhesus monkeys try to get food, such as peanuts, they activate individual cells in the prefrontal motor cortex. The same cells are activated when the researcher tries to get peanuts - while the macaques are sure that this gesture was deliberately made, and that the experimenter plans to eat this nut. Since the same cells are activated both when initiating an action and when observing an action, they are dubbed ‚Äúmirror neurons‚Äù; and the entire neural network is called the ‚Äúmirror neuron system‚Äù.

Since macaques made a distinction between whether a gesture was intended to eat peanuts or play with them, the researchers stated that the mirror neuron system was able to determine intent, and that monkeys were also able to understand someone else's mind. In the following decade, mirror neurons were harassingly hated as the neurological basis of empathy, complex social interactions, language evolution, and cultural development characteristic of modern humans. The neurologist-behaviorist Vileanur Subramanian Ramachandran even found it possible to assert that ‚Äúmirror neurons will do for psychology what DNA discovery did for biology. Marco Jacoboni, a neuroscientist at the University of California at Los Angeles, a pioneer in working with mirror neurons, said the system worked at a basic level of recognizing simple intentions and actions - just about how we could work in a high-stakes poker game. You are about to bet, and suddenly notice that the player on the left is preparing to push his stack of chips. He can make this gesture specifically to distract you from some aspect of the game. Despite the inadequacy of these leading explanations of the MUF in neuroscience, it is still difficult for us to give up the belief that we can look into the mind of another person.
