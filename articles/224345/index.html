About the correlation and not only


Sometimes, having data on hand, you feel the lack of standard tools. This is especially the case when the numbers are a dynamic process that constantly strives to change the internal state.
Under the cut, the author will try to show how, using a simple trick, from ordinary data you can pull out mountains of various information. In these mountains, you can find the most intimate details of the process under study, the only question is in curiosity and a certain amount of luck.

We take two simple curves as the initial data - the average speeds for some categories of roads in a couple of cities of our vast Motherland.

Just for information, we see a cut of data for the week in 15-minute increments.

What could be done with standard tools? Go through the moving average, build the distribution, find the variance, co-variation / correlation, make the convolution. This will give us a few numbers, a few new curves. And if this is not enough to understand what is actually happening there?

Recall that we have two intervals on our hands, each of which can give a bunch of sub-intervals. Comparing the two sub-intervals in turn can give us a number (or numbers). Choosing ways to generate the sub-intervals of the two original curves, we begin to generate at least two-dimensional data. For example:
  • Fix the size of the sub-intervals
  • On the abscissa axis we will postpone the distance from the beginning of the interval to the beginning of the curve.
  • The ordinate is the same, but for another curve
  • The value will be considered the correlation of two sub-intervals
