Tomorrow (Aug. 6), at 9:20 am (inaccurate time), the autonomously-controlled Nasa vehicle, called Curiosity, will land on Mars.
Planting a device so far from the Earth is difficult because a person cannot interfere in the process, or rather, it can, but most likely, will be too late. From us to Mars, the radio signal goes 14 minutes.
Thus, it is not easy to sit on the planet correctly, with a very thin atmosphere and a huge layer of cosmic dust on the surface. Scientists had a task to plan this landing from the beginning to the end, and this is how they solved it:

While Pathfinder, Spirit, and Opportunity landed on Mars, literally, with the help of cushioning balloons, Curiosity will use the largest parachute and support platform ever created, which will gently deliver the device on cables to the surface the planets.
