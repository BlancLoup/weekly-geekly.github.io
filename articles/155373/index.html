In France, they want to force search engines to pay for content. Google threatened to remove French sites from its index

The ingenious socialist government of France proposed to adopt a law according to which search engines indexing the content of sites located within a country would have to share the proceeds from advertisements that usually appear along with search results.

The French Publishers Union is lobbying the government in the government for this bill, which is caused by a well-known complaint about users who prefer to find free content on the Web instead of buying a newspaper or a paid version of an electronic journal. And in search they are well helped by various search engines (in fact, Google), greatly facilitating the task and bringing considerable headache to the publishers of newspapers and magazines in the form of loss of income from the sale.

The search giant sent an information letter to the French ministers in which he expressed his disagreement with the planned law and threatened that if he was accepted, all French websites that would have to be paid for indexing would be excluded from Google search results. Given the fact that, thanks to Google, four billion visitors a month go to the relevant sites, such measures will actually mean the collapse of the business of many companies that are focused on the Internet.

Google also clarifies that, although the need to pay for the placement of links to sites, seriously affects its finances, the end users, for whom France will disappear from the virtual space on the one hand, will suffer the most from possible forced measures, and on the other hand, The government is not going to charge for access to certain content (which is quite possible and understandable), but only for a link to it, which will adversely affect the freedom of information on the web.

It is also not inconceivable that the recent problems of Google in Europe, which consist in the fact that the company is being required to seriously change the privacy policy, is due to the influence of France in the European Parliament, which is trying, therefore, to continue its tough policy on the Internet and tough anti-piracy laws .
