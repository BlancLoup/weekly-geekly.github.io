At the Web 2.0 Summit this week in San Francisco, Mary Meeker, Mary Queen of the Internet, according to Barron's Magazine, announced the list of network development trends for the near future.

Meri Meeker is one of the most influential analysts of the IT sector, a partner in venture capital firm Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers from Silicon Valley. Investors monitor its annual reports, relying on them in choosing a strategy. Her first report in 1995 marked the beginning of the dot-com boom and radically changed investors' views on IT and web development.

The list proposed by Mary Miker is as follows:

  1. Globalization
  2. Mobile technology
  3. User interface
  4. Online shopping: fast, easy, interesting, cheap
  5. Online advertising
  6. Content creation has changed a lot
  7. Technology Lead - USA
  8. Mega-trend: empowering through mobile communications
  9. Economy: a lot of uncertainties

The report notes that in the 20th largest Internet companies in the world there are more and more companies from China, Japan and Russia, which over time will only come on the heels of the Americans.



Large Internet companies are now not only in the USA: the rest of the world is also gaining momentum.

Despite the fact that the United States continues to lead in the number of Internet users in percentage terms, the growth rate of the Internet audience in China, India, Nigeria, Russia and Iran is much higher. A few years ago, no one would have thought that these countries, according to the rate of growth of Internet users, would occupy leading positions in the same rating. And these are new audiences, and, therefore, new markets, which have to be reckoned with.



According to the statistics given in the report, an average Russian user spends 9.7 hours per month on social networks. This is about 30 minutes a day. With this indicator, we occupy only 5th place, while the United States went to 12th position. The top three are Israel, Argentina and Turkey.

Such a gap can be explained by the presence of a huge amount of illegal content in the domestic social networks, the viewing of which takes the lion's share of the users' time. While law-abiding Facebook users use social networks for their intended purpose.
