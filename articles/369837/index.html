The cloak of invisibility




Harry Potter studies the properties of nanophotonics (almost)

The invisibility mantle is one of the most popular magical artifacts, wandering from a fairy tale to a fairy tale for hundreds of years. Now we can confidently say that invisibility like Harry Potter is achievable with the help of metamaterials streamlined for light rays. A metamaterial is a composite whose properties are caused not so much by the features of its constituent elements as by the artificially created periodic structure. In metamaterials there is a negative refractive index in the visible region, thanks to which you can hide a three-dimensional object.

Xiang Zhang from the University of Berkeley published an article in Science stating that his team was able to achieve invisibility in the entire infrared spectrum for a three-dimensional object. The invisible layer of metamaterial 80 nanometers thick consists of a gold substrate, gold nanoantennas and magnesium fluoride. Antennas have a unique property: they can resonate with the light falling on them and then scatter it. Nano-antennas can change the phase of light, while maintaining its intensity. With the help of these antennas, light is given such characteristics as if there was no object under the ‚Äúmantle‚Äù.
