IoT devices - the favorite target of DDoS attacks

Viruses are parasitic on a growing number of Internet of Things devices, while their owners may not even be aware of this. The number of viruses aimed at the Internet of Things (IoT) has increased many times over the past year. In 2015, a record number of attacks was registered, with 8 new virus families registered. More than half of all attacks originate in China and the United States, but the number of attacks from Russia, Germany, the Netherlands, Ukraine and Vietnam is also constantly growing. The low level of security on many Internet of Things devices makes them easy targets, and device owners are often unaware of their infection.

Only this month it became known about a large DDoS attack launched from 3 different types of botnets (surveillance botnets , home routers and infected web servers ). According to forecasts, the number of such attacks using devices Internet of Things will steadily grow.

Attacks on devices of the Internet of things predicted for a long time, most often it was about home automation devices and home security. Most vulnerable devices


The most common targets of intruders are embedded devices. Many of them can connect to the Internet, but due to operating system limitations and processing power, they usually do not contain any additional security features.

Embedded devices are often once connected to the power grid, go through a simple installation process, after which they are forgotten. For such devices, no firmware upgrades are performed, and they are subject to replacement only at the end of their life cycle. As a result, any compromise or infection by viruses goes unnoticed by the owner, which is a sweet lure for intruders.

China and the United States - the birthplace of most attacks


Analysis of Symantec, which resulted in the collection of many malware samples, showed that the largest number of attacks originates in China, which accounts for 34% of all attacks in 2016. 26% of attacks come from the US, followed by Russia (9%), Germany (6%), the Netherlands (5%) and Ukraine (5%). Vietnam, Great Britain, France and South Korea close the top ten.

The most common threats


While malware for IoT devices are becoming more sophisticated, the fact that they are mainly used for DDoS attacks allows us to highlight several common features that can be traced in the variety of existing malware.

The more widespread the virus, the more straightforward the attackers. While some malware must be manually installed on a device, the most common method is to scan random IP addresses with open Telnet or SSH ports and then try to log in using frequently used credentials.

Due to the different architectures of the processors on which embedded devices work, malware can try to randomly download bot executables for several architectures and run them one at a time until the scheme is successful. In other cases, the malware can also include a module that checks for the platform of an existing device and downloads the correct bot files.

The usual tactic is to use wget or TFTP commands to load a script (.sh), which in turn loads the bot files. In one of the cases, a shell script was used where street drug names were used to distinguish bot binaries for different architectures.

Fig. 3. Shell script used to load binary bot files for different architectures

When executing the bot files, a connection will be established with the C & C server, awaiting commands from the remote master bot . Communication can be established via the IRC channel , and malware can also include functionality for encrypting traffic to a remote server.

Cross Platform Vulnerabilities


Cross-compiling different architectures is a fairly common fact. While the most common targets are the x86, ARM, MIPS and MIPSEL platforms, attackers continue to expand the number of potential targets, and also create options for PowerPC, SuperH and SPARC. Thus, the list of potentially vulnerable devices is growing, and more and more web servers , routers, modems, network storage devices, video surveillance systems and other devices are being added to it.

One interesting feature: many malwares are seen in the ability to kill other processes, in particular processes belonging to other viruses. In some older versions, this feature was used only to eliminate potential competitors from an infected device. The most common reason is that embedded devices come with very limited system resources, and the malware is trying to free the processor from "extra" procedures.

To achieve the same goal, but on the basis of a more complex approach, malware can also modify the rules of the IP table on an infected device so that only certain types of external access can be attempted. Such changes make it possible to effectively block access to the device for other intruders, as well as to block the actions of admins (blocking the telnet port).

DDoS attacks remain the main target of viruses targeting IoT devices. With the rapid growth of the Internet of things, an increase in computing power in devices may cause changes in tactics in the future, and attackers will have new opportunities for the extraction of cryptocurrencies, theft of information and network espionage.
