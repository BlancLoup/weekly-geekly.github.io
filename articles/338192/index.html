Experiment: Is everyone really good at design?



Everyone has an opinion about design. "This is a bad logo." "This is a good logo." "This design is not worth so much." There are plenty of such comments under any design news. How can people who are not professionals be so easily able to draw conclusions from a single glance?
I decided to conduct a small experiment to check whether everyone is really so well versed in design, as they think.

Poll: why is the Polska Moto logo so successful?




To understand whether people can really draw the right conclusions by looking at the design, we created a survey. We asked our subscribers to describe the impression of the unknown Polska Moto logo in Russia.


This company, contrary to expectations, incredibly quickly took a leading position in the market of motorcycles. Their logo in Poland can be seen in every motosalon:



Surprisingly, the majority really understood why a company with such a logo came to success. In their answers, I read many flattering words about the successful combination of shapes and colors, for example:

This is very nice, especially considering that this logo was made personally by me in a couple of minutes. But I'm not even a designer! Nevertheless, 57% of respondents tied the success of my fictional company Polska Moto with the logo. They were looking for reasons in the combination of colors, similar to Microsoft, simplicity and non-standard:

Which answer is correct?




It is clear - the majority saw what they wanted to see. Someone realized that the logo was not real and had us back in response. But I want to know the answer - is this a good design or a bad one? Would a company with such a logo be successful or not?

A quarter of all respondents answered that there is no connection between the design and development of the company. Maybe this is the right answer?

In fact, in our question no more sense than in: ‚ÄúWhy did a company with such technical support become successful?‚Äù. Or: "Why did a company with such a head of the supply department go bankrupt?" A lot of clarifying questions are needed here: ‚ÄúAnd how much did technical support play a big role?‚Äù, ‚ÄúAnd how did the supply department go?‚Äù. And only with the help of a mass of additional information can we draw not only conclusions, but simply modest assumptions.

Let's return to the design: Logoshin's team made hundreds of logos for various companies. We have seen projects become known or disappear without a trace. But neither we, nor anyone else will tell you how the logo design and the success of the company are connected. What would have changed if the Apple logo had not been bitten by an apple? Nobody knows. What if Apple left her old logo? Everything would change, but no one will say exactly how.



The logo is an important part of the identity. Identity is directly related to branding. But there are firms that have achieved success, without building a brand at all. And there are brands that attract customers with their design, stand out among competitors and sell goods at times more expensive. Focusing on branding and design is a matter of strategy. For some companies it is useless, for others - the key to success. And for most, this is just a big plus, as a quality website or a solid office in the center.

Therefore, the correct, from our point of view, answer is: ‚ÄúNobody in the world knows what role the logo design played in the success or failure of this company‚Äù.

findings




The design is very little objective. If we asked: ‚ÄúWhy is this program not working?‚Äù, Most would honestly say: ‚ÄúI don‚Äôt know, I‚Äôm not a programmer.‚Äù And the developers would open the code and find an error, if there is one.

But when we ask: ‚ÄúWhy this design does not work?‚Äù, - everyone has an opinion. And this opinion is very easy to influence.
Therefore, all the focus groups and polls ‚ÄúHow do you like my logo?‚Äù Are not consistent - people with a smart look argue about what no one understands at all. The same signs can be interpreted in two ways - in front of you is a simple and memorable logo or is it simply primitive?

You can only say "I like the logo" or "do not like." But even here it is very easy to influence the impression - at public presentations of design, colleagues most often look at the boss and endorse him. And sometimes the boss is waiting for a ‚Äúhint‚Äù from colleagues, because he does not understand that there is a high-quality design or low-grade clipart in front of him. Then the first voiced opinion sets the tone.

A simple example - the client was disappointed in the logo, because, quote: "Showed to all friends, nobody liked it." Everything would be fine, but he showed the design with the words: ‚ÄúI drew some shit, what do you think?‚Äù. The conclusion from the whole story is simple: most of the opinions about good and bad design are based only on subjective feelings. And sensations are created not only from the sense of taste and prejudice, but also from the "halo effect" - the opinions of others and other factors.

Therefore, every time you see comments about "this logo is not worth so much" or "it was better before," you know - you are amateurs. Professionals are silent, because they know that they do not understand this.
