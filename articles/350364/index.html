It seems that the gestalt approach to psychotherapy and flexible development methodologies are very close. This article is an attempt to unwrap this thought, meaning habra readers. What is agile here should be clear, but what psychotherapy in general is and the gestalt approach in particular certainly needs clarification. Having dealt with both of them, you can look at familiar things from a new angle and, as always, understand something about yourself, about the world and something else.

Psychotherapy, unlike psychiatry, is a treatment by talking. Without pills. Or, to put it mildly, the art of chatter. If we talk about violations from the diocese of psychiatrists, then most often, in different proportions, both are needed. For example, in case of exogenous depression, antidepressants are first prescribed so that the patient who is exhausted has the strength to undergo therapy, then the psychological attitudes that drive him into this state are disentangled, and drug support is gradually canceled.

And psychotherapy is not medical. Thoughts that something is wrong in their lives, but what exactly is not clear, come to healthy people. Household advice, such as: "Yes, okay, do not worry, - or, - I wish your problems" - only add fuel to the fire.

Therapy is not training. Training is the development of skills, therapy affects the level of the individual, the level of conditions for the application of these skills. If trainings provide a suitcase of tools for use in negotiations, during public speeches, in conflict situations, then therapy, especially gestaltist, is more about finding support in such situations. It may well be that once the support is found, no additional suitcase of skills is needed. Moreover, if the problem is deep, trainings will not help at all. The person in a critical situation will be confused and stupidly will forget the learned skills to apply.

Gestalt therapy is client-centered. If in the psychoanalytic therapy the analyst acts as an expert and in some sense repairs the patient, in the gestalt approach the client himself is the leading expert in his life.

One of the significant themes of the gestalt approach is the paradoxical theory of change. Changes do not occur when a person tries to become what he is not, but becomes what he is.
