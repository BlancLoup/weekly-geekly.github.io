About the book


Judging by the reviews, experts all over the world consider Gavin Kennedy's work the best textbook on conducting professional negotiations. The material is presented in an interesting game form, replete with practical examples and life situations. According to Kennedy, negotiators are divided into 4 categories:

  • Donkeys - guided by blind principles, do not analyze the situation, ignore valuable information, stubbornly stand on their opinions and, as a result, often lead to negotiations at a dead end, disrupting the deal.
  • Sheep - humbly agree to any conditions of the opponent, make unilateral concessions and enter into unprofitable contracts.
  • Foxes - understand well what is happening, gather facts, are distinguished by pragmatism and resourcefulness. When making a transaction, the fox takes into account only its own interests.
  • Owls are wise enough to appreciate long-term benefits. Analyze information, looking for all possible ways to enter into contracts that benefit both themselves and the second side.


Of course, this is extreme, and often people combine the character traits of several of the listed characters in different situations. Before each chapter we are waiting for a fascinating test, the results of which we fall into one of the categories. I thought that I had a great deal of experience in negotiating and would like to associate myself with an owl, but as it turned out, sometimes I behave like an ass and sometimes like a sheep.

Oh, if this book fell into my hands 3 years ago! But even now, just a few months after reading it, the time I spent paid off was an order of magnitude. It is remarkable that Gavin Kennedy writes very briefly and to the point. He does not like to pour water, unlike Kiyosaki or Adizes (whose work I, of course, have great respect), so the book does not have a single ‚Äúextra‚Äù text.
