Discussion of completed test tasks


In the last part, I described what I already did two test tasks: the first on DevOps Engineer, the second on Ruby Developer. I'll tell you what happened next.

Interview with Ruby Developer - the interviewer didn‚Äôt even look at my test case, didn‚Äôt ask any questions about it, didn‚Äôt compliment (I completed the task better than all previous candidates, at least the recruiter flattered me so much). It seems that he did not even know about him. It upset me a little, because then they began to ask me a theory, and, as a result, through theory they refused.
Interview on DevOps Engineer - the interviewer looked at the assignment, made a compliment (said that I had done it well) and asked a few test questions on the decision: why is this line here? As the recruiter told me, some candidates did not do the tasks themselves, and could not answer such questions. So here I managed without problems.
In the first case, I did not ask the interviewee whether he even looked at my test task and what he thinks about this. And it was necessary! If you have such a situation, be sure to indicate to those who are interviewing you.

Tasks for interviews


Let's continue the topic of the test tasks from the previous part and consider the tasks for coding at interviews.

Tasks can be of several types: implement an algorithm, write a solution in pseudocode, update something, and so on. Our engineers are most fond of algorithmic problems.

Many people, including myself, believe that these tasks show only the ability of the candidate to solve such problems, and they do not at all show how he will cope with real work tasks, which are usually higher-level. Why do they still give them? I think the reason is simple: interviewers are simply not able to come up with something better. And since we cannot think of anything better, let's take the good old inversions of strings, sorting, balancing trees, and so on.

As tools that are used to solve, the code editor + repl + collaboration capability is best suited. Of all the options on the market, I like the CoderPad the most . A room is created where you and the interviewers connect, you can edit, execute code and look at the results of the implementation together. Very comfortably. If there is no money for the codepad, then repl.it and sharing of the screen goes into battle - basically the same thing, only without the possibility of collaboration.

Upd: while the article was being translated, repl.it added Multiplayer Mode, which allows an unlimited number of participants to work simultaneously with the code. Absolutely free! CoderPad is no longer needed, hooray!

I contacted a Zoom technician and he says: ‚ÄúLet's start with an algorithmic task.‚Äù I answer: ‚ÄúOk, I‚Äôll do the task, but I have a question: do you need this knowledge in your work, do you need to solve similar things?‚Äù. We opened the codepad and proceeded to the task, the condition of which was voiced verbally to me: ‚ÄúFind the longest possible sequence of unique characters in a given string‚Äù. After this, the interviewer said that "one could give a task for dynamic programming , but we confine ourselves to a simpler option." I would like to look at the person who will give a dynamic programming task at interviews, seriously.

I repeated the task conditions to the interviewer to make sure I understood her correctly, to which I received an affirmative answer and began to work. After 5 minutes, it turned out that I understood the task nevertheless incorrectly, because it was necessary to find not the substring, but its length (this is easier). However, I replied, "Well, once started, let's solve the problem with an asterisk, not a simple one." The interviewer seemed satisfied, and then began to ask me about the data structures. It turned out that to solve the task that he wanted to give initially, it would be possible to use a hashset, or something like that, and he was expecting just such a solution, but I broke it all.

Further quite a bit talked about the project (forms and krudy). And then he says: ‚ÄúAfter this, there will be an interview about system design. The interviewer agreed with me, but he turned on the delegation of responsibility again and said: ‚ÄúIt‚Äôs not me who decides what the process should be, so we‚Äôre doing what we‚Äôre doing.‚Äù I will add that the second interview (on system design) I did go through and it was as meaningless as the first.
What mistake did the interviewer make - he did not fix the conditions of the problem in the text. When I conducted such interviews, I simply copied the conditions of the problem into the code editor so that the candidate would have no questions left.

I made a mistake - I immediately rushed to write the code without specifying three times whether I understood the condition correctly. If you are given such tasks at interviews, then immediately disconnect from the chat and find yourself a more interesting lesson. Check several times to see if you understood the condition correctly, write a test in the editor and get confirmation from the interviewer that everything is correct.

‚ÄúIt didn‚Äôt work,‚Äù I thought, and told my interlocutors: ‚ÄúGuys, you know, I‚Äôm good at web making and doing projects, but with your stupid tasks I‚Äôve got something wrong. Typical deficiencies in interview tasks


  1. The task does not concern the real work. It annoys me the most. Allow to solve the algorithms, although in reality the crudes are riveted. Let's take the relevant task, I will make you a cruise! Why do you need a person who can search for strings in strings?
  2. Organizationally - often there is no normal tool for solving. Once I was shown the code in google docs, once I fumbled the screen in repl.it, once was CoderPad.
  3. The task does not create a context for further conversation - this is a consequence of the first paragraph. Why give a task if we don‚Äôt discuss it later?
  4. Not all people can cope with the task under supervision. Accordingly, a good candidate can be eliminated at this stage.
