US farmers are still trying to win the right to repair their own John Deere tractors

In order for the owners to repair their tractors, you need to change the US copyright law




Photo: oticki / Thinkstock

About a year ago , an article was published on Geektimes about an American farmer, Dave Alford, who has worked on his own ranch for many years. The total area of ‚Äã‚Äãthe ranch is about 400 hectares. For processing all this land, Alford uses a large amount of equipment that breaks down regularly. Dave prefers to repair everything that has failed independently.

For three decades, the rancher has repaired more than a dozen tractors. Not so long ago, he bought another one - a modern John Deere 8520T tractor. There is air conditioning and even on-board computer. It can show, for example, the state of the engine. If something fails, the computer immediately shows what kind of system is broken. It is very convenient - you can fix everything, knowing where and what needs to be repaired. But this is where the convenience ends, since the farmer does not have the right to repair the tractor himself.

The fact is that the software of the on-board computer of the tractor is protected by the manufacturer. The farmer has no right to crack DRM digital protection (although Alford knows how to do this, he says), since he will immediately be subject to the 1998 Digital Millennium Copyright Act of 1998. For violation of the DMCA, the court may order the farmer to pay half a million US dollars or imprison him for several years. So far there have been no such precedents, but everything is possible.

That is why, with every tractor breakdown, Dave has to wait for a company specialist, or take the equipment to a service center, which is 65 km from the ranch. All this time work is worth it.

It is clear that farmers do not agree with this point of view, believing that once a tractor is bought, all rights to it belong to them.



The conviction of farmers that they should have the full right to repair their own equipment, is supported by many representatives of other professions - lawyers, technicians, electronics specialists. But support in words is one thing, and lobbying interests is another. Someone was supposed to lead the team protecting the rights of farmers. This man was Kyle Wiens, founder of the iFixit community.
