Why do we need certification for people who work in Agile?


For each, of course, your answer. In general, certification is usually needed for the following reasons:


  • In order to check how much your knowledge coincides with the expectations of the industry. Very often, managers say that they are well versed in Agile, because they have a good managerial background. Certification ruthlessly looks at your knowledge and experience and will tell you whether you really are a guru or you need to grow in some areas.
  • In order to pass the employers filter. In IT in general, and in the Agile community in particular, not much attention is paid to entities and certifications. And yet, very often when looking for employees, the first filter can be a certificate. This is especially true of two types of companies - integrators who like to show certificates of employees in response to the question of high outstaffs, and bloody enterprises, where large enterprises are willing to pay many times more, but that in addition to knowledge would be beautiful certificates, love for dress - code, excellent English, etc. Whether it is necessary is only for you to decide, but if so, then get ready for the fact that certificates are needed at the first stage, or at least you will have to answer the question about their absence.

In general, if you are an accomplished specialist, then either you know better than me what certification to choose, or you no longer need any certifications and there are queues behind you from Madrid to Beijing. But many famous coaches, coaches and edzhal-leaders have certificates, and not one by one. This makes me think that most likely certification is not superfluous.

Scrum.org


general description


In my opinion, quite complex certification, focused on classic scrum, while taking into account the principles and values ‚Äã‚Äãof Agile. Scrum.org has also established itself well around the world, the certificate will facilitate the search for work in Europe and in the countries of the former CIS, training courses are also common.


For us at the moment 6 tracks are supposed - 3 of which are organized by the roles of the Scrum team - Professional Scrum Master, Professional Scrum Product Owner and Professional Scrum Developer. The remaining three - Scaled Professional Scrum, Professional Scrum with Kanban and Professional Agile Leadership will not be reviewed today, but focus mainly on PSM. PSM (like PSPO) has three levels, one more difficult than the other. In the first, you need to answer questions, quite a lot of which are practical, in the second, the questions will be much smarter and pass without special training and much experience most likely fail, well, and in the third, you must explain each question with text that the coach will then review scrum.org. The second and third levels are more necessary if you are going to become a coach, but the first one is a very good proof that you are not a stranger in a scram and understand the terminology and rules of the game.


The trick of the test is mainly that there are not very many questions on the theory, but there are plenty of practical questions. At the same time, the decision must be made fairly quickly, 80 questions are given in just an hour and by the end of the test there is usually no time for review. At the same time the threshold of entry is quite high - 85% of the answers should be correct.


The good news is that right on scrum.org there is a free open assessment test that anyone can pass. The certificate will not be given, but you can understand the level of preparation for the test (but do not expect that if you have passed it, then everything is OK, the real test is much more complicated).
