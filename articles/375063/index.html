Apogee was founded in 1987 by Scott Miller, and the first game published was the Kingdom of Kroz arcade game, which used pseudographics in text mode on the screen ... but sales, oddly enough, went well. In 1991, he was joined by George Broussard, who published a number of his old games on behalf of Apogee, and in the future was responsible for the Duke Nukem series of games.

One of the main ideas tested by Apogee is a new version of shareware-distribution of games. Prior to this, the company had already tried various distribution options: traditional, in the morning money, and in the evening chairs. And a variation on the topic of donations, when the game was distributed free of charge, and for the money technical support, tips on passing and cheats were bought.

In shareware, the player received for free the first episode of the game, containing, as a rule, approximately 10 levels, and the subsequent ones were already acquired for money.

[live inset] However, in this place someone will probably remember the toys Beyond the Titanic and Supernova. Indeed, both of these toys were developed by Scott Miller and were published by Apogee, but they were also published before the creation of the company. And the Kingdom of Kroz was the first game that was published immediately by Apogee, and not by someone else. [/ live insert]

And there were variations too, for example, initially the games could be bought in an episode, well, i.e. let's say buy only 2 or only 3rd and 4th episodes. Later this scheme was abandoned, and the game had two options: shareware with the first episode and a full version with all the episodes. This method allowed to fully get acquainted with the game before buying it, which is much better than the current situation, when usually even demo versions for games are not made, and all that remains is video clips and reviews.

In 1995, the company created the 3D Realms trademark, under whose name they planned to develop and produce three-dimensional shooters. The plan was to create several brands, each of which would publish games of its own genre. But as a result of the change in the market, they gave up on this idea, and only 3D Realms and Pinball Wizards were created, on whose behalf only one game was released - a pinball called Balls of Steel.

It should be noted that Apogee was mainly a publisher, and much less often a developer. Although games such as the Duke Nukem series were developed on their own, in most cases they were the publisher. How do you like Max Payne? Also published in Apogee! More precisely, already 3D Realms.

Moreover, Apogee was a ‚Äúgood‚Äù publisher: it did not require exclusive rights from developers, although it didn‚Äôt provide any special monetary support until the end of development, being limited to deductions from actual sales. In short, she was an indie games publisher even before it became fashionable. And the concept of INDI did not exist at all ...
