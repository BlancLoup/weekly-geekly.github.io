From a conversation with Al Baron


Polk Audio's SurroundBAR project manager agreed to say a few words about soundbars. In principle, he said two really interesting things - I will retell them here.

The mechanism of the system‚Äôs normal operation, according to Al Baron, is:

The system receives a 5.1 Dolby Digital sound, and then down-mixes this sound to two channels, adding HRTF effects on the fly in such a way as to create the illusion of five speakers.

Curiously, however, the behavior of the system when working with an initially two-channel source, for example, music from a CD. By the presence of differences between the channels, the system will try to build the highest possible surround image by adding HRTF effects.

As a result, we get some surround effects - although they will be worse than listening to the same material recorded in 5.1 format.

A similar task, the so-called. In principle, artificial spatialisation has long been known and its various solutions have been proposed for more than 30 years in scientific papers and hardware. However, the whole technology of soundbars is, in fact, a new approach to a well-forgotten old projectile ‚Äî much more successful than attempts from past decades.

The second interesting point is the opinion of the company Polk Audio about the possible competition between multi-column systems and sound bars.

I expected to receive, in response to a question about the development prospects, the words that traditional systems would be supplanted by soundbars - and I was pretty surprised by the answer.

According to Al Baron, such systems will never be able to catch up with good multi-column systems, but for many customers (mostly unable to clutter the room with speakers or intending to use the system behind a computer) will be a reasonable solution, allowing you to get as close as possible to the multi-column home theater.
