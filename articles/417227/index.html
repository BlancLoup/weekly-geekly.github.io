What happened to the Fermi paradox

The Fermi paradox lies in the fact that the probability of an extraterrestrial civilization is usually estimated quite high, and there is nothing to see signs of its existence. Recently, a preprint by Sanders and co-authors ‚ÄúDissolving the Fermi paradox‚Äù appeared on arxiv, which has already been interpreted as the abolition of this paradox (true), idle talk in the absence of data (most likely, but true for the Fermi paradox in general, and not just for this article) , and as evidence of the non-existence of aliens and / or low L (false). In this article we will try to figure out what the preprint actually contains.


What's the matter?


First of all, the Drake equation itself. It is presented on the KDPV; the last factor is a joke, and the rest should be understood as follows: in the observable universe (or our galaxy, or any other space) there is stars Every star with probability has a planet. Each of these planets with probability is in the Goldilocks area, has a livable chemical composition, etc. On every potentially life-sustaining planet with probability (per unit of time) life arises, which with probability achieves rationality and probability gets in touch. And finally, every rational species that emerges exists years from birth to extinction. If you multiply it all, you get a number of reasonable species in this area.

The time between the birth of the mind and the appearance of a civilization noticeable in the interstellar distances is neglected. In the case of the Earth, for example, billions of years passed from the appearance of the planet to the appearance of life and from the appearance of life to the appearance of the species Homo sapiens . Technological progress is incomparably faster - the emergence of Homo sapiens is less than ten million years from the message of Arecibo. Interstellar colonization has not yet begun, but this is also, in the worst case, a matter of a couple of million years. Compared with pre-technological stages - within the margin of error.

Which, how to say it more practical, is quite high . From the order of magnitude for the number of stars and the probability that they have planets up to 200 orders of magnitude (two hundred orders of magnitude, the estimates differ in times) for the probability of abiogenesis. The ungrateful thing is to evaluate the probabilities from a single observation. However, scientists published various estimates and most often came to the conclusion that or even . What, in fact, is the Fermi paradox: according to our best estimates, there should be other reasonable races in the galaxy, and there is no reliable evidence of their existence.

The problem is obviously not in the galaxy: if the estimates differ from the observed reality, then so much the worse for the estimates. Either one of the parameters is greatly overestimated ( and God bless us if it is L ), or we somehow incorrectly believe. Sandberg et al. Protect the latest version.

It would seem to multiply a handful of real numbers - not Bin Newton. The catch is that in such a situation, the multiplication of numbers relies on the assumption that they are somehow known to us. As we saw above, this assumption is very far from reality.
