Review of the Compaq SLT / 286 laptop. Retro from the 80s

Surely a lot of people have some favorite old things. Someone just obsessed with them, and keeps the whole museums of ancient technology. And besides, it often sounds that they used to do better, the sun was brighter and the grass is greener. The quality of the old technology can be argued for years. However, this introduction, for lovers of old iron, I suggest this review of the laptop.

Story.



In 1988, Compaq launched its first personal laptop with a VGA card on board. Characteristics of the machine:
  • 8 "5 inch black and white screen with 256 grayscale
  • 80286 processor at a frequency of 12 MHz
  • 20 MB hard drive
  • 1.44mb floppy drive
  • 640 kilobytes of memory which then will be enough for everyone
  • 1 VGA, 1 COM, 1 LPT, 1 External Floppy / Streamer ports
  • the price is only $ 5399.




Funny money. For the same ridiculous $ 599, a modem capable of 2400 baud could be installed in a laptop, but without MNP. It was still possible to increase the memory, right up to 12 MB, by installing three additional modules of 4 MB and put the hard drive at 40 MB (and then at 80). It was also possible to buy a docking station into which 2 ISA cards fit. Operating time from one charge was 3 hours. (If interested, you can read the 1988 article on the New York Times )




This handsome man fell into my hands at the beginning of 2001, and he was in a dead state, with a dead hard drive and a floppy drive that reads almost nothing. Without a native power supply and battery, with a dead modem and hard drive, and, as it seemed at that time, a dead bios (I learned about Floppy Setup Diskette, I learned to use Floppy Setup Diskette a couple of months later). Iron.



This laptop has an original keyboard - it is removable, and with a full PS / 2 connector. For a while, cheap noname Chinese noname was exploited instead - everything worked fine. Alas, time leaves its marks, and the plastic of the keyboard in some places has changed its color.


Inside, the laptop is amazing - the whole structure is assembled on an aluminum chassis. All blocks are shielded from each other.


If you remove the installed blocks, and unscrew the screen, then you can get to the motherboard.


One strange detail is visible here - some kind of wire and a piece of electrical tape. People who came across the old equipment with the ‚ÄúDallas‚Äù on board probably already guessed what it was and where it was going. For those who do not know what Dallas is, this is NVRAM (CMOS) + RTC (Clock) + battery that powers all this when the power is off, all this is flooded with compound. Just the battery in this module dies in 10-15 years (12 years manufacturer's warranty), respectively, the laptop settings and the clock are reset every time the power is turned off, but for desktop motherboards this is still nothing, set up quickly going into the BIOS and working, and generally you can score. But brand-name + laptop played a cruel joke - the CMOS settings, when the battery is discharged, are reset even with a cold restart (not to mention a power outage). And on the computers (up to the Pentium-III), to enter the CMOS it was necessary to boot from a special diskette (then their number reached as many as three and the configurator worked under Win 3.1), and after the configuration was completed, that same cold restart went. Vicious circle. The correction of this mistake is visible in the photo, the Dallas neatly sawed the compound at the location of the battery terminals, and a wire was soldered to the NVRAM / RTC feet to the new connector.


The laptop has a very specific floppy drive with a 20-pin connector. Other hardware does not shine with standardity either, everything has Compaq connectors, except for a hard disk.
