Musical DIY shaitan machine or what did a Swedish musician with two thousand balls

Once again, the creation of musicians served as a pretext for publication. This time they were the Swedes from the instrumental group Wintergatan. When I saw that the permanent leader of the team, Martin Mulin, and his colleagues had built, I realized that it was worth writing about it. A mechanical semi-automatic acoustic instrument, called the Marble machine, is amazing. Skeptics express the idea that this cannot work at all. The inventiveness of the author, the ambitiousness of the project and the courage of the plan border on the best manifestations of creative madness.



Few details


The difficult and painstaking work on the tool began about 3 years ago and lasted for 14 months. By February 2016, the Wintergatan had completed their work on the Marble machine. The presentation video published by the group blew up YouTube and currently has over 40,000,000 views.



It should be noted that the team is not the first time creating interesting mechanisms, as can be seen by looking at their numerous videos.

For example, this one, where Martin plays a huge music box with a punched tape and some wild variety of the Termen cello called Modulin



Interestingly, in childhood, the creator of the machine, Martin Mulin, loved to draw various mechanisms. The musician's children's hobby has not passed with age, now he not only draws them, but also builds them successfully. The thing in question in this post exceeded even the wildest expectations of fans of the group who are used to the extravagant tricks of Mulin. The principle underlying the machine is as follows:

music is played thanks to the interaction of 2000 balls with percussion instruments, bass guitar and metallophone, which are mounted on a wooden frame.



A complex mechanism set in motion by the muscular force of the musician‚Äôs hands (like a barrel organ) forces balls to move, which fall on instruments in a certain order and extract sounds from them. It looks like this - the person turns the pen drive, the balls fall, and a melody appears. The order of the falling balls is determined by a special "software" wheel, acting like a roller of a huge music box.





Drum (software wheel) indicates that the creator of the machine seriously studied similar on the principle of the device of the 18th - 19th centuries. This is evidenced by other inventions of the Mulin, as already mentioned "box" with a punched tape and a mechanical piano, with which the musicians also experimented.

Although it may be better to just watch the video:


I think that many will agree with me, the performance of the melody on such an instrument is truly a fascinating spectacle.

Numerous pedals, keys and levers are used as controls for the combination instrument, and ordinary ground rice is used to create some percussion sounds. In total, more than 3,000 moving parts were used to create the machine. The vast majority of parts of the device were cut on a CNC plywood machine.



The vintage look of the tool reminded me of an animated movie I first saw several years ago. The video showed a surrealistic non-existent automated stringed and plucked instrument with a large number of fingerboards.


It is possible that this well-known online video was one of the sources of inspiration for the musician and inventor.

About how exactly the machine works, Mulin told in detail in the relevant video:




Interestingly, for high-quality sound reproduction the machine is equipped with a large number of microphones installed in a special way (they allow to hide the noises of the operating mechanism and amplify the sound). Switching occurs through the mixing console, which is also an integral part of the instrument.



As the inventor himself said in one of the interviews, the instrument was conceived as something like a mechanical DJ installation, which allows combining the performance of a previously recorded melody and making changes in it in real time.
