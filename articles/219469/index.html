In the late 60s of the 20th century, Lawrence Peter put forward an interesting idea: "In any hierarchical system, an employee seeks to achieve the level of his incompetence . " In order to understand this, it suffices to consider a typical employee movement algorithm along the career ladder. Usually, when a new job appears, the manager selects a candidate who has positively manifested himself in the current position. Promotion is usually associated with a change in the nature of duties performed. If the employee copes with the current position - he becomes a candidate for a higher one. In such cases, the captain of evidence says: "Competent employees go up."

Sooner or later, an employee reaches a position in which he will not be able to cope with his duties, that is, he will be incompetent. This is the level of incompetence of the employee . In such a situation, the return of the employee to the previous level is unlikely, since the lower position is usually occupied, and the management will have to admit their management error. As a result, although the incompetence of the employee in the new position is obvious, he is not lowered. Thus, we get the following corollary: ‚ÄúIncompetent employees do NOT go down.‚Äù

" .

Since any organization consists of employees working in it who have a certain level of competence, a new concept can be introduced: the general level of the company's competence. It is logical to assume that the higher the general level of competence of a company, the more competitive and more efficient it is.

Thus, the Daning-Krugerav effect in conjunction with the Peter principle leads to a disappointing result: in any hierarchical organization, the overall level of incompetence tends to increase over time, and organization, as a system, degrades .
