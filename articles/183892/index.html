But first, some background information.


We reworked the controller layout a bit. The Wi-Fi module, which used to be on the main board, moved to a small shield (we call them mezzanines). This is done for greater versatility. And, again, not everyone needs a wifi by default. And because of the placement on the mezzanine, it immediately became possible to change the modules without touching the main controller board.

MicroSD, which in the first version was on a special adapter (it was for debugging), has finally moved to its main place on the main board.

USB has now become a full OTG (in the first version there was only a USB-host).

The main display LED (bright, three-color) was replaced with three LEDs with a slight glow, and earlier in the dark the LED actually blinded the eyes :-)

Ethernet has become "external." If earlier the main percent was responsible for the grid, now a separate small chip is responsible for it. This was done because of the acute shortage of interruptions - the network subsystem used to occupy almost all the interrupts of the probe, so they decided to make a feint with their ears. As a result, interruptions became higher than the roof, and the grid is there. On the main board they decided not to install the RJ-45 connector (they didn‚Äôt do it on the first version either), since I did not want to pay a fee. In addition, as practice has shown, the network connector on the controller board is inconvenient, since it is difficult to get close to it when the controller is inside a device - it is not always possible to plug a piece of iron, for example, into a tank so that the connector fits to the back of the case so that you could take him outside. In general, for me personally, it was sooo uncomfortable. Therefore, I proposed to make the RJ-45 remote, on a loop. There are special pins in the comb of the pins, so that the connector is placed anywhere (on the back wall of the box, on the bottom of the tank, on the back wall of the robotic arm, etc.) and connected to the pins on the comb. From the nuances - the regular LEDs of the RJ-45 connector, we do not carry out network indication in the usual sense. Those. If you plug in the cord into the connector and into the switch - the LEDs on our connector will not blink! But (when connecting the appropriate wires from the connector cable), it became possible to make a software ‚Äúhigh-level indication of the network status‚Äù directly on these LEDs. The user's software, which is responsible for direct control of the virtual work logic (for example, control of a robotic arm), blinks an LED at its discretion. For example, the user connected to the manipulator - you can light the green LED on the connector, there is a connection to the server - light the yellow one. Etc. Changed the power connector. Instead of screw terminals (they were inconvenient in practice) they made an ordinary small connector. It became more convenient to connect.

Some rarely used pins were removed from the pin comb, and GPIO was added along with them. Also comb now with "protection from the humanities." Previously, the comb was symmetrical, the second floor could be put on incorrectly. Now it will not work out the wrong way (although if from the heart it is possible to push it with all your heart).

Camera modules and Wi-Fi modules are now also in line for production. In general, the production process itself is quite fast.
