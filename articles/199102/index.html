How to find a developer in a garage startup: from personal experience

Are you an entrepreneurial enthusiast, and you have an idea that will change the world? Congratulations! And now here's your first challenge, which will quickly bring you down from heaven to earth. Unless you are a lone genius, you will need a development team. And if you really want to realize your idea, they must be professionals. Or at least one of them, the most important one - again, if you yourself have not been a professional service station since 1978. And now the question is: how to attract into the garage start-up without an investment a seasoned developer who weekly tries to entice multi-million corporations, each of which is willing to pay him n thousand dollars?


Initially, our project team consisted of three people - a project manager, a journalist and a financier. Everything is fine, but there was no one to code. So we had to search.

Money


It is believed that the main goal of a start-up is to raise investments, mold a product version that works for good (albeit on many crutches) and sell it to Google for a fortune. Based on this, we can assume that for the members of the startup team the financial component - read salary - should be very important. And immediately and preferably more.
But the garage start-up is a cashless default, since there is no product yet, investments are not raised, and all the money goes to pay for the services necessary for the work. And to compete for employees with a large development company, where salaries start at $ 2 thousand, a startup is often not able to do.
At one time, we solved this problem simply: we went to the regions. Found a wonderful developer-downshiftera, escaped from the capital to the next area, and invited him to his. He liked our idea, and he agreed to work for the minimum bid. In his sleepy town, it was still boring and nothing to do.
However, as soon as he began work on the project, the town suddenly stopped being sleepy. When it turned out two weeks later, he was able to complete only one-fifth of the tasks assigned to him, we had to seriously talk. He explained that he was invited to take Java courses, and he was embarrassed to refuse. In addition, he received an extremely interesting offer, which he simply could not refuse, and he only had about two hours left for our project. The problem was that about two or three weeks he worked in this mode, while we thought that he wrote our service all day and night. His recognition and report on the work performed were an unpleasant surprise for us. We parted.

So we realized that you can't buy motivation with money, because there will always be someone who will give more money, especially if you are in the garage.

Share in the company


Two days after breaking up with the first developer, we found a new one. This new most of all resembled electrobroom. For a start, he proposed to organize a four-day hackathon, during which we had to fully implement the basic functionality of the service. Hackathon failed, but we still agreed.
This developer did not need money. He wanted fame and share in the company. And considerable - a third. For this, he had to write the engine for our web service alone and take almost all of his support for the first time. We gritted our teeth, but agreed. The deadlines were tight - by the end of the month we planned to make a presentation at the largest meeting of psychologists. Where we, of course, did not have time.
It so happened that we actually ran into the same rake. The last place of work of our developer was an IT company with foggy Ukrainian owners in gold chains and managers who formulated their desires roughly as ‚Äúwell, so that here, it means the button was, well, such that the screen blinked, and something else from above stick it on. " As a result, a huge development team actively chaos every day from ten to six. And this, obviously, has become a habit with our developer.
Taking on the role of the future service station, he immediately brought a lot of rustling. He could call in the middle of the night asking how best to implement this or that feature. Or convene those same hackathons, which, however, the case stopped soon enough.
But three weeks before the scheduled conference, friends from his hometown came to visit him, and for a week he turned off the process.
And immediately after leaving friends, he fell ill. Sick one and a half weeks. At the same time, it turned out that in the previous time, in spite of the active demonstration actions and dozens of tasks initiated in the task tracker, almost nothing was done. We called him to account, but he announced that he was dying from angina. After his recovery, we met, he was really pale, but at the end of the conversation he blurted out that the cause of pallor was not a disease, but a hangover. The next day he had to start working on a new section of the service. But not started. He didn‚Äôt answer our calls anymore, although in certain places someone was constantly seeing him.

So we realized that motivated enthusiasm does not mean real interest. Our developer perceived the project as a hobby, a passing hobby, because he was old and wise, and had certain notions about how to work: not to get a result, but to satisfy (or imitate satisfaction) the authorities. It was definitely not what we needed.

Moreover, obviously, the financial and personal interest that made him claim a share of 30% was in the too distant future to motivate a fairly practical person every day.
