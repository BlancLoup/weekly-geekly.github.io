What was interesting on DataVizDay in Minsk

On Thursday, October 4, I attended the DataVizDay conference in Minsk as a speaker. I will share the most interesting ideas and impressions of Misna.


Key ideas:


  1. 80% of your efforts will be up to BI and visualization, because the data is either bad or very bad and basically you will spend time preparing and collecting data.
    2. However, visualization creates the value of your product date. Without visualization it turns out just a bunch of numbers.
  2. Unfortunately, very often visualization is bad, bad approaches are used, types of graphs and histograms overload views with details. As a result, we often see Kill by powerpoint and the abundance of data does not add transparency to the analytics.
  3. Excel continues to occupy a significant role in the processes. And often companies are not ready to move on to something advanced. But even on Excel you can build a lot of interesting things, because a good analyst probably starts with cleanliness and data preparation, and not with beautiful dashboards.
  4. From business cases - using data on social networks to build a map of ‚Äúthoughts‚Äù about parts of the city. For example, in Moscow, all tweets about the center, and nobody writes about life around the center. It is as if he is not in the social space, although most of the inhabitants of Moscow live there.
  5. More traditional cases are end-to-end analytics from contact to sale. True, so far no one has come with a case study not just through analytics from the contact, but given the fact that the client may have many contacts and the last contact that led to the purchase is not the most reliable pointer to the channel of attraction. So while all of our end-to-end analytics is somewhat skewed by the excessive importance of the channels and the underestimation of the fact that people may have many contacts with the brand.
  6. There have been a lot of discussions about bad visualizations and how to fix them. I learned that people are abusing a pie chart and a whole block of one of the presentations was set aside to overcome this problem. Pie charts are suggested to be changed to column, sankey diagrams or to replace lines altogether.
  7. Two paradigms struggle - the data analyst should only poke buttons and the data analyst should be able to write code (R / python, SQL) for his visualizations and data processing pipeline. Personally, I am a supporter of the second approach, something like the DevOps paradigm, only in analytics, when a technical issue should not be an obstacle in the delivery of an analytic product.
  8. Many people with whom I spoke often complain that they have never built a convenient data storage and have to finish a lot of things in Excel. This is despite the fact that they have many bearded Itshnikov, who saw this storehouse for years.

Whatever the data and storage of their incorrect extraction, interpretation and processing means fake analytics. And since the data is becoming more and more democratic, then each user must somehow receive minimal training in order to use this data. But often in companies, things are not so good with this preparation, training and documentation on the use of storage. I can only say that working with maps and their visualization is really interesting and important for managing a company.
