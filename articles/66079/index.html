Letters of Biel

A little inspired by the article about Kryptos , I decided to tell you about another unresolved encryption task today.

So - America, 1885. A brochure appears on sale, on 23 pages of which an unknown author publishes the history of Biel and how to find his treasures.


In January 1820, Thomas Jefferson Beale settled in a hotel in the city of Lynchburg, Virginia, which was owned by Robert Morriss (Robert Morriss).
These two are known to us participants in this story.
Three months later, Biel leaves the hotel, but in January 1822 he returns and also leaves in March.
However, this time Biel leaves Morris for a locked metal box. After some time, he receives a letter asking him to give it to the person who comes after her with a power of attorney from Beel, if Beale himself does not do it earlier. If this does not happen after ten years, Morris should open the box and read the letter addressed to him, as well as the paper, for which you need a key. The key never came, and Morriss opened the box without it in 1845.
Inside, he discovers a letter telling the story of Biel and his friends, as well as three sheets of ciphertext.

The letter says that Biel and his 29 friends in 1817 embark on a journey through America. Once during a buffalo hunt, they find a small ravine about 300 miles from Santa Fe, where they discover gold and begin its prey.
After two years, the question of shipping the mined gold closer to home in Virginia arises, and all the partners agree that the gold should be hidden.
In order to hide the gold Biel and appears for the first time in Lynchburg.
His second arrival was marked by the replenishment of the cache.

Companions are also concerned that in the event of their death, gold will never be found, and it will not go to their families.
It is with the purpose of conveying the location of the treasures Biel and gives the box with the encrypted text to Morris, because he considers him an honest person.
The letter talked about the meaning of sheets with ciphertext - in the first sheet it was indicated where the treasure was hidden, in the second sheet the list was hidden, and in the third it was said about who this treasure should go to.

After studying these sheets, a friend of Morrisss realized that the text could not be encrypted using the usual replacement cipher, because the numbers used in the ciphertext are larger than the letters of the alphabet. And then he suggested that the so-called book code was used here.

The meaning of the book cipher is that the key, which encrypts and decrypts the message, is the book, or part of it.
To do this, we take the book and enumerate all the words in order. Then there are two options - the number will correspond to the whole word, or just the initial letter.
(There is another option when the word is encrypted with three numbers denoting the page, the line, the place of the word in the line in the key book.)
The advantage of such encryption is that without having a key, you cannot read the message, and the message is not subject to frequency analysis.
The downside is that the key books must be absolutely identical, and that the books must somehow be delivered to both parties.

So, a friend Morrissa suggested that he was dealing with a book code and tried to pick up a book.
