Scientists in Japan and the United States announced the launch of clinical trials of a new anti-aging drug



Medical scientists from the University of Washington, St. Louis, USA, and Keio University, Japan, are conducting final preparations for the launch of large-scale clinical trials of a new drug, the main purpose of which is a significant extension of human life. In accordance with the preliminary plans of the project managers, the start of testing is scheduled for early-mid-July, however, according to the requirements of Japanese law, the Green Ethics Committee should be given by Keio University Research Ethics Committee.

At the stage of clinical trials, the effectiveness of the drug and its safety for the human body will be evaluated. Studies of the newest drug, based on the substance of nicotinamide mononucleotide (nicotinamide mononucleotide, NMN), have already been tested on animals and demonstrated significant positive changes in the dynamics of the aging process. It is reported that during testing, this drug will be administered to ten adult and healthy volunteer people.

In the course of the experiments that have already been carried out, the research group, led by Gerontology expert Professor Shinichiro Imai from the University of Washington, was able to show that the substance NMN activates the genes responsible for the production of sirtuin protein, known for its rejuvenating effect on the body and called the ‚Äúelixir of youth‚Äù. As shown by a series of experiments with laboratory mice conducted by a group of Professor Imai earlier, the sirtuin protein actually demonstrates the ability to push back age-related changes, manifested in a decrease in metabolism and vision. The results of the experiment with laboratory mice, more

In the course of their experiments, scientists investigated mice ‚Äúprogrammed to produce‚Äù sirtuin, while in some of the mice such overproduction occurred throughout the body, and in others only in certain tissues. Here, scientists have come to a curious conclusion: as it turned out, in order to increase life expectancy, it is crucial that sirtuin be active in the brain area. Interestingly, the increase in lifespan in this case was exactly the same as in normal mice hooked on a low-calorie diet. But if the level of sirtuin in the brain increased, the mice could eat anything and anytime: their lifespan was extended anyway.

Further studies showed that the lengthening of life was accompanied by a complex of physiological and behavioral features: the mice were more active at night (that is, when they were supposed to be active), their body temperature and the volume of oxygen consumed increased, their muscle tone improved, fell asleep faster and slept tighter. Finally, these changes, as well as a tangible increase in the lifespan, were observed in both males and females. According to Mr. Imai and his colleagues, the longevity of females increased by 16%, in males - by 9% . If you correlate with a person's life expectancy, then, accordingly, a woman with such changes in the body may well live to be a hundred years old, and the average "minimum" for men, without much stress and diet, will reach eighty.

Scientists also noted that in mice with a high content of sirtuin in the brain, the threshold for resistance to cancer was also higher. As for the key issue - aging, here it is very important to note that a shift in the moment of the start of the aging process was recorded, but its rhythm did not stretch in time . Simply put, aging itself did not slow down, but it started much later. Subsequently, in this regard, sirtuin dubbed the "source of youth."
