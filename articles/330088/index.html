All Flash Isilon NAS: Scalable Storage for Unstructured Data

Until recently, the potential of IT infrastructure was constrained by dependence on mechanical hard drives. But with the advent of flash memory, companies had the opportunity to increase the speed of data processing and improve the way they are stored.
Flash memory is faster than a hard disk, but the scale of its impact on the data center ecosystem is even wider. Due to its high speed, it reduces the time during which other elements of the ecosystem expect a response from the repository. This increases the efficiency of applications, servers and data networks.

According to analysts of the Enterprise Strategy Group , increasing efficiency with the same performance reduces the cost of a number of components of the IT ecosystem ‚Äî server hardware and software licenses, and also reduces the total cost of ownership and maintenance of the data center.

This makes flash memory a profitable acquisition, even at a high price. But the price factor is also losing its strength: there is a steady downward trend in the cost of solid-state drives in the industry. Flash memory has become more accessible, and companies have the opportunity to introduce new systems with high-performance data access.


In 2016, flash drives caught up in price / capacity ratio with a 15K enterprise-class HDD.

The idea of ‚Äã‚Äãa completely diskless data center has real features. If, until recently, solid-state drives were assigned the role of a tool for high-performance transactional workloads, today, cheaper flash memory has expanded its scope to a wide range of tasks.

The respondents to the ESG survey on data storage trends predict growth in sales of storage systems over the next 24 months due to business intelligence and big data analytics applications.

Today, the topic of using flash drives in data centers is the number one in the industry. First, experts note a tendency to gradually replace traditional rotating disks (HDDs) with solid state drives (SSDs). This helps companies improve the reliability of storage systems and reduce costs in different areas of their service. After all, the recording density is growing rapidly: the newest flash memory is eight times higher than the recently released drives. Secondly, the explosive growth in data volume doubles the storage requirements every two years, and 80% of this new data is not structured.

The value of performance in working with unstructured data


Processing unstructured information using business intelligence or big data analytics in real-time requires the performance of the AFA technology level (all-flash array) - arrays entirely built on flash memory. Such storage systems are designed specifically for the needs of unstructured data.

Today, there is a demand for access to low-latency unstructured content in the media, entertainment, design automation (EDA), research, etc.

The latest ESG survey of IT managers responsible for data integrity in companies showed that 49% of respondents already use solid-state technology, another 20% are expecting the deployment of flash systems in the next 12 months. The majority of respondents confirmed that as a result of using systems on flash memory, companies received an efficient IT ecosystem and reduced operating costs. According to the survey, other advantages of solid-state drives are increased efficiency in the use of resources, reduction in operating expenses and lower cost of ownership.


Flash storage systems improve application performance and resource efficiency. Reduce energy consumption, reduce operating costs and equipment costs, improve SLA parameters and reduce TCO. Source: ESG survey.

An ESG survey showed that 44% of companies improved their total cost of ownership, and buying flash drives was a sensible acquisition. By reducing the price of solid-state drives, modern technologies have become more accessible for a wide range of workloads and data types, including unstructured ones.

Deployment of systems on flash memory, the introduction of large capacities for structured or block data requires a lot of money, which is very heavy for many companies. Therefore, flash arrays for unstructured data should be initially well-scalable. This will make the initial investment acceptable, especially given the gradual decline in the cost of flash memory components.

But hardware is only part of the solution. Large companies will need the functionality of an enterprise-level file system for efficient operation: support for multiple protocols, automatic data migration across storage levels (tearing), and a horizontal-scalable architecture.
