5 million accounts have already been registered in ProtonMail cryptographic mail.

In 2013, employees of the European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN), with the participation of colleagues from Harvard University and MIT, developed ProtonMail , an encrypted mail service. He is in Switzerland. According to the developers , the service is protected from the actions of American and European laws, primarily from the control of the NSA. The main goal is to ensure security and respect for the privacy of users. In a sense, ProtonMail was a response to the closure of Lavabit cryptomail after the attack of the American special services on its creator, Ladar Levison . The Swiss company Proton Technologies AG is not subject to US law, which minimizes the likelihood of such a scenario.

In September 2018, ProtonMail reached a significant milestone: 5 million registered users . ‚ÄúWe want you to be able to completely de-gleify your life,‚Äù said co-founder and CEO, Andy Yen, in an interview with Inverse . - ProtonMail provides all the [necessary] functions, as well as security and privacy that Google cannot provide. This is our long-term vision. ‚Äù In addition to mail, encryption of documents, spreadsheets and everything else will appear in the future.

Open beta testing of ProtonMail began in 2014 . ProtonMail messages are not stored openly on servers, but are encrypted on the client side. Two passwords are used for authorization: one for logging into the account and downloading encrypted data, the second for decrypting. Password recovery for decryption is impossible; if it is lost, the correspondence cannot be recovered. At that time, ProtonMail was called the only mail service to which the NSA could not access.

According to Yen, from January 2017 to September 2018, the number of ProtonMail accounts increased from 2 million to 5 million. ProtonMail and related ProtonVPN (free VPN) are faced with opposition not only from major Internet companies. Periodically, servers are subject to DDoS attacks , and in some countries this access is blocked at the state level. For example, in March 2018, the Turkish authorities blocked access to protonmail.com. Turkish telecommunications companies use DNS poisoning , disguising their servers as Google DNS resolvers, as well as other methods of blocking.
