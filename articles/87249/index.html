A little about the principle of the screen

Here we should mention another class of vessels capable of floating above the surface of the water, the so-called hovercraft. They found application in military science and were produced both in the USSR and in foreign countries. Here the screen effect is used, but it occurs due to the air injection under the bottom, which has a special shape. Such vessels are capable of speeds up to 120 kilometers per hour and it is easy to overcome small shoals, overgrown reeds and similar obstacles. One of these ships was the Soviet "Lynx", equipped with three engines: two of them set in motion propellers, and the third forced air under the bottom.

The ekranoplans also use the screen effect ‚Äî a change in the wing carrying properties at low flight altitudes ‚Äî discovered by aviators back in the 1920s. Since the flight data of the aircraft, and, in particular, its stability, were not designed for this effect, in some cases it led to accidents and catastrophes of aircraft during take-off and landing modes of motion. Apparently, one of the first domestic works devoted to the influence of the earth on the aerodynamic properties of a wing was the experimental work of B.N. Yuriev (1923). In the period 1935 - 1937 A complex of experimental and theoretical studies in this direction was conducted by Ya.M.Serebriysky and Sh.A.Biyachuev at TsAGI. Around the same period, a number of theoretical studies were conducted by prominent foreign scientists: A. Betz, K. Weezelsberg, S. Hagget, D. Bagley, M. Finn. The results of these studies allowed us to give a qualitative assessment of the effect of the screen effect on the aerodynamic characteristics of a low-flying wing. In particular, it was shown that the lift force of the wing grows, and the more the closer the wing is to the ground; resistance decreases, the longitudinal moment changes. This made it possible to develop appropriate recommendations for the control of the aircraft, in which the influence of the screen on the aerodynamic characteristics in takeoff and landing modes is manifested.
However, for aviation, this effect continues to be "harmful."
