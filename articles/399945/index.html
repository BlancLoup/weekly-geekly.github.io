The rise and fall of home-made ideas

The company MakerBot made a bold bid that 3D printers will become as popular as microwaves. There is only one problem: all the others did not share her enthusiasm.

Petis, a former art teacher, became a key character in the growing market in the late 2000s, the global community of hobbyists doing their own hands at workshops, workshops and hackerspaces, as well as at home, and working with classical lathes and modern laser cutters. Petis began his ascent in 2006, recording weekly videos for MAKE magazine ‚Äî self-made bibles ‚Äî during which he performed such foolish tasks as connecting light bulbs to a hamster wheel feed. In 2008, he co-founded the NYC Resistor hackerspace in Brooklyn. By the time he was already a star. A year later, he launched a startup with friends Adam Mayer [Adam Mayer] and Zach Smith [Zach Smith] called MakerBot.

‚ÄúWe had a unit that produces three-dimensional objects, and it was awesome,‚Äù said Petis from the scene of Ignite NYC. Grabbing technology from the size of large machines worth $ 100,000 to desktop boxes, MakerBot launched a revolution in 3D printing. With the help of a 3D printer, objects developed on a computer were physically formed in three dimensions by overlaying the layers of molten plastic on each other. And now any MakerBot owner could design and print his objects.

From the point of view of Petis, the consequences of this were amazing. People printing objects at home could go to the store less often and do whatever they want. He shared a story about ‚Äúprinted happiness‚Äù: a certain person needed a ring to make an offer, and he printed it out. For five and a half minutes, Petis praised what he said was the ‚ÄúIndustrial Revolution 2,‚Äù which MakerBot led.

‚ÄúYou will be a producer for yourself,‚Äù he said. And he completed his presentation by asking the listeners to "make their own future."

A year before MakerBot was founded, analysts predicted that the global market for 3D printers, with a volume of about $ 1.2 billion , would double by 2015. By the end of 2012, he almost grew up. MakerBot seemed to be on time: that year the company released its most famous and probably the best device, the Replicator 2. MakerBot predicted that the device would appear in thousands of houses. Petis has become a cult character. Even before the launch of the company, according to Stalts, "we already watched everything he does, watched the weekly videos and watched his projects." With the release of MakerBot, he became the king of hackers.

But the second industrial revolution, and the times when every-self-maker, armed with new ideas and loyal MakerBot, never came. By 2015, Petis, Meier, and Smith were engaged in other projects. This year, the device from a Taiwanese manufacturer has become the most popular desktop 3D printer.

How did MakerBot, the darling of the 3D industry, fall so low and so fast? Petis does not respond to requests for comment, and Smith and Meier declined to be interviewed for this article.

We gathered information from various industry observers, today's MakerBot management, and a dozen former employees of the company. Some introduced themselves, others wanted to remain anonymous.

Over the years, MakerBot needed to make two successful moves. It was necessary to show the wonders of 3D printing to millions of people, and then convince them to pay more than $ 1000 per machine. She also needed to develop a technology that was fast enough for customers to be satisfied. And these tasks were beyond the power of the young company.

‚ÄúMakerBot made major commitments and tried to satisfy market demand, which never appeared,‚Äù says Ben Rokhold, who worked as an engineer for four years at MakerBot. In search of a dream about how everyone would become a manufacturer, MakerBot tried to produce printers that were both inexpensive and attractive, but they didn‚Äôt succeed.

At the very beginning of the way, the community, rallied around these printers with an affordable architecture, thrived on the MakerBot base.

The inspiration came from the British professor Adrian Bowyer, who began working on RepRap, a desktop 3D printer in 2005, using layer-by-layer fusion modeling to print objects. Bower wanted to create a printer that could print a copy of himself. One RepRap would spawn another, printing parts, and so on. In New York, three friends, upon learning of this, came up with their own idea. Can they make a machine that prints the parts needed to assemble a RepRap? And the answer was positive. Working at NYC Resistor, Zack Smith, Adam Meyer and Bra Petis created CupCake CNC, a machine that could print parts for RepRap on request.

‚ÄúThe idea was to make a small number of such printers, thanks to which people can make their own RepRap, and the first batch sold very quickly,‚Äù said Stalz, who worked at MakerBot from December 2011 to April 2012.

MakerBot personified the revolution in the production of things. The demand for CupCakes was so great that the owners of these devices even helped MakerBot print out the details for sending out new kits. On Google Groups and Reddit, active forums thrived, fans shared their design of objects on Thingiverse, the site launched by the company and stored files under a Creative Commons license.
