A bank experiment: air purifiers against dirty money

In the spring of 2015, we, together with a large Russian bank, conducted two air purification experiments in banking offices in Moscow. These were pilot projects: they must show how expedient it is to use our purifiers-recirculators in bank offices. Now we are ready to tell you what we finally got. We cannot disclose the name of our partner, therefore we will call it simply the Bank.



The purpose of the first project was to reduce the incidence of bank branch employees, the second - to reduce air dustiness in the cash collection center (CIC) from manipulating a large amount of money. For this, Tion cleaners were installed in five branches of the Bank and in one of its CICs.

Why air purifiers for bank employees


Tion air purifiers with HEPA filters retain all types of pollutants, including biological. They reduce the number of germs in the air, and with it the risk of catching an infection.

Take at least the flu. We did not manage to find detailed statistics on the incidence in Russia, but individual studies [1] show that during the epidemic about 1% of the adult population of Russia is sick with the flu. 1000-1500 clients pass through one Moscow branch of our Bank per day. It turns out that during the epidemic season, employees can daily contact with 10-15 patients. And this is just the flu. There are still other acute respiratory viral infections and other diseases that are transmitted by airborne droplets.

In addition, bank employees can become infected not only from customers, but also from money. For example, there is a study [2] in which 94% of one-dollar bills turned out to be carriers of pathogenic microorganisms.



The ruble in this sense is not far behind the dollar. And according to some sources [3], even overtook him. The results of a selective analysis of banknotes from the Moscow market at the Yaroslavl station showed that, on average, potentially dangerous pathogenic bacterial and fungal microflora was found on all banknotes. And the dirtiest turned out to be paper denominations of small denominations. They most often go from person to person and more often get dirty.

Pathogens can live on money for up to four months. Any greasy or sticky spot on a bill is an excellent breeding ground for germs. By the way, bank employees can easily be convinced of this: dirt stains on banknotes are clearly visible under the ultraviolet detectors that are used in banks.

With frequent use, paper notes collapse and paper particles enter the air. Not only is the harm from the dust itself, so along with it all the ‚Äúmoney‚Äù germs fly into the air. And the more money around a person, the higher the probability of catching an infection. Now imagine how many bills every day passes through collection centers and bank offices. Automatic bill counter goes through the banknotes with great speed. After each such operation, he releases into the air a cloud of ‚Äúmonetary‚Äù dust with microbes.

Potentially sick clients and money dust - because of these two factors, it is hard to work, and to go to the hospital is easy.

Why air purifiers for bank management


For the bank itself, ill employees are a serious problem. First, the payment of sick leave is a direct loss to the employer. Yes, the employer pays only the first three days of sick leave, but still it is a loss. And if an organization of a federal scale with a huge number of employees, in the sum of all offices, even three days of sick leave can be a serious cost.

Secondly, employees at the hospital - this is lost income (especially when it comes to managers or managers responsible for the most profitable products).

Another benefit for the bank is the reduction of the so-called replacement staff. Any bank has a swappable staff for the smooth operation of the branches in the event that the main employees en masse leave for sick leave. This happens, for example, during seasonal outbreaks of disease. Usually, the bank‚Äôs policy does not provide for temporary hiring of staff only for the period of the outbreak. More often the bank has a permanent ‚Äúreserve‚Äù of swing employees in the state. How many banks do you need? It depends on how many employees go to hospital during outbreaks.

If the air purifier in the office reduces the incidence of it during seasonal outbreaks, then it will be necessary less swing workers. The average salary of a bank employee is 50,000 rubles per month, including taxes and deductions - about 70,000 rubles per month, or 840,000 rubles per year. For example, a bank‚Äôs expenses for employees from a replacement fund are 2‚Äì3 times less - it‚Äôs still several hundred thousand rubles a year. That is exactly what a bank can save if it cuts at least one swap employee.

Finally, an employee with ARVI often suffers a disease on his feet and continues to work in the office. He works, as a rule, worse than in a healthy state. In addition, it can infect colleagues and further increase the loss of the bank.

Finally, air purifiers in the office can enhance the image of the bank and customer loyalty. After all, this is a concern not only for employees, but also for visitors to the bank.

Conclusion


Air purifiers will help employees feel better at work and less likely to get sick, and bank management will help reduce costs. It remains to measure these effects in practice.
