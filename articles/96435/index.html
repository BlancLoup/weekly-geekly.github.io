Information on what Habr's readers and users are doing can be gathered from two sources: the questionnaire and the Thematic Media media kit. The result tells us that a typical developer in the former USSR combines programming with many other tasks: from traditional system administration and layout to exotic ones like merchant fleet management or furniture production. Nearly two thirds of habravchan work "on uncle." More than a third (36.6%) of users combine different types of activity, for example, work and study or work and freelancing.


A third of users consider themselves to be experienced professionals. Slightly more than 14% indicated that they are engaged in managerial functions. This indicates a high professional and expert community level. (Want to argue? I ask in the comments.)


But why do these people visit Habrahabr, and sometimes they also write topics?

The study showed that Habr is considered primarily an educational news resource. Entertainment materials are less interesting.

As it turned out, in the overwhelming majority of cases, this is a simple desire to share knowledge, to communicate. Not many people want to become known or increase the level of karma, a little more than a quarter of those surveyed. Often, among the incentives for creating topics, people distinguish invites.


What about karma? It would seem that this axis around which all Habr turns. However, research shows that people treat her calmly.
