WHO recommends


The World Health Organization recommends that you contact your general practitioner once a year and at least conduct a general examination and check blood pressure, blood sugar and lipids, and body mass index. These very basic recommendations will identify at an early stage cardiovascular diseases, hypertension, diabetes and others. WHO provides a minimal list of examinations - in the expectation that the results of screening will be just enough to check the most common diseases.

But some do not agree


One of the arguments against check-ups is the insufficient evidence base that complex examinations improve quality and longevity. This is true - regular examinations are just beginning to enter the usual clinical practice, and so far not enough time has passed for the results of the checkup to prove themselves in the long term. A modern person is accompanied by many technologies, the harm or benefit of which is not yet sufficiently studied - each of us uses a smartphone, although there is no data on how this affects a person. Returning to the check-ups, it should be noted that the harm from examinations is also not proven - but the forecasts so far speak about the benefits of early diagnosis of diseases.

Another argument of criticism - surveys without indications lead to overdiagnosis. As a result of screening, problems that do not require active intervention can be detected. In this case, the doctor may begin treatment that does not help the patient, does not potentially improve his quality of life, or even harm him. Indeed, a prophylactic examination helps to identify small deviations from the norm that do not carry any risks, especially if the patient does not worry about them. Here the doctor plays a key role: he must interpret the test results and decide which actions are necessary. It is the doctor who weighs the potential risks and benefits of any intervention and selects the desired treatment option (or inaction) on the basis of evidence.

Screening begins with a visit to the therapist who examines and interviews the patient and makes a detailed history. These data help our doctors to conduct sighting examinations and better navigate the organs and systems that are worth paying attention to.

Analyzes




Biochemical blood test is needed to assess the functions of vital organs and systems.

Total protein, bilirubin, alkaline phosphatase, gamma-glutamyltransferase (GGT), ALT and AST - all of these indicators reflect the state of the liver and the inactivation system of toxins.

A blood test for cholesterol and other lipids (LDL, HDL, triglycerides) is used to assess the risk of atherosclerosis, stroke and heart attack in the future. Prothrombin indicates blood clotting and the risk of thrombosis.

C-reactive protein (CRP) is an indicator of inflammation. The level of uric acid is needed to assess the metabolism of purine bases: violations can cause deposits of uric acid crystals and joint pain, kidney damage. Urea and creatinine levels reflect kidney function. The content of glucose in the blood helps to identify violations of carbohydrate metabolism.

Complete blood count supplements the information obtained and reports data on the level of hemoglobin, red blood cells, platelets, leukocytes and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR). These indicators may indicate iron-specific anemia and the overall level of inflammatory processes in the body.

If necessary, the therapist may prescribe a urinalysis - to further diagnose the condition of the kidneys and urinary system, if there are signs of inflammation or patient complaints.

Tests for tumor markers are not included in the mandatory program of the check-up and are appointed only if there are indications. We do not consider tumor markers a means of early diagnosis of oncological diseases, they are necessary to exclude relapses in the presence of an aggravated history.

Research




According to the results of the patient's primary conversation with the therapist, the functional diagnosis doctor will know what to look for during an ultrasound of the internal organs . Duplex scanning of the great vessels will help check the condition of the arteries, assess the risk of atherosclerosis and thrombosis, and choose the appropriate prevention options.

Electrocardiography is necessary for screening severe heart disease. By the way, the Netherlands physiologist Willem Einthoven received the Nobel Prize in medicine in 1924 for the discovery of electrocardiography.

It should be noted here that by themselves, the results of an ECG or ultrasound scan have no value until they are described by the doctor. This avoids overdiagnosis and informs the patient only really important information.

Changes may be associated with diabetes mellitus or arterial hypertension.
