New approach to building the tree of life


When the British morphologist George Jackson Mivart [St. George Jackson Mivart] published in 1865 one of the first evolutionary trees, he lacked supporting material. He built a tree - a branching map of various species of primates - with the help of a detailed analysis of the spines of animals. The second tree, created on the basis of comparison of limbs of animals, showed other related connections between primates, highlighting the problem of evolutionary biology that still exists today.

Almost 150 years later, scientists acquired a mountain of data to build the so-called phylogenetic trees , a modern version of the structure created by Mivart. Advances in DNA decoding technology and bioinformatics make it possible to compare sequences of hundreds of genes, and sometimes whole genomes, in different species, and create the tree of life with more detail than has ever been possible.

But although the abundance of data has helped to resolve some of the conflicts that have arisen over various sections of the evolutionary tree, it has brought new difficulties. The current version of the tree of life looks more like a controversial Wikipedia page than a published book ‚Äî there are constant controversies about some of the branches. Just as the spine and limbs have led to the emergence of contradictory primate evolution maps, scientists now know that different genes in the same organism can tell different stories.

According to a new study, partly based on the study of yeast, the controversial picture drawn by individual genes is even more controversial than expected. "They claim that each of the 1070 genes is involved in a conflict," says Michael Donoghue , an evolutionary biologist at Yale, who is not associated with the study. ‚ÄúWe are trying to understand the phylogenetic relationships of 1.8 million species, and we ourselves cannot sort out twenty types of yeast,‚Äù he says.

To resolve the paradox, researchers developed an algorithm based on information theory to measure the level of confidence in the correctness of individual parts of the tree. They hope that the new approach will help bring clarity to the periods of evolution, possessing both the most interesting and useful and the most conflicting data - for example, the Cambrian explosion - the rapid diversification of animal life that occurred 540 million years ago.

‚ÄúHistorically, the most interesting episodes are connected with the areas that attracted attention and caused controversy,‚Äù such as the origin of animals, vertebrates, and flowering plants, says Anthony Rokas , a biologist at Vanderbilt University who led the new study.

Bricks of life


The basis of phylogenetic trees is created through the grouping of species according to their degree of kinship. If we compare the DNA of humans, chimpanzees and fish, it becomes clear that humans and chimpanzees are closer to each other than to fish.

Researchers once used one or more genes to compare organisms. But in the last decade there has been an explosion of phylogenetic data that very quickly filled the bases necessary for the creation of these trees. The analysis made it possible to fill in a few of the white spots scattered around the tree, but serious disagreements still exist.

For example, it is still unclear whether snails are closer in relation to them - bivalve mollusks or shovel-legged mollusks , Rokas says. It is not known how exactly one of the earliest branches of animals from a tree are connected, such as jellyfish and sponges. Scientists can demonstrate examples of conflicting trees that appear in the same scientific journals with a difference of weeks, or even in the same issue .

‚ÄúHence the question: why is it so difficult for us to come to an agreement?‚Äù Rokas says.



Rokas and his graduate student Leonidas Salichos studied this issue by evaluating the genes separately , using the most useful genes ‚Äî carrying the most information related to evolutionary history ‚Äî to build their version of the tree.

They started with 23 yeast species and selected 1070 genes. To begin with, they created a phylogenetic tree in a standard way, by concatenation. To do this, all the sequences from individual species come together in one megagene, and then the sequences of individual species are compared with this long sequence, on the basis of which a tree is created that best explains the differences.

The resulting tree is accurate in terms of standard statistical analysis. But since similar methods lead to the appearance of trees full of disagreements, Rokas and Salichos decided to delve into the subject. They built sets of phylogenetic trees for individual yeast genes, and applied an algorithm, developed using information theory, to search for the areas of greatest fit between different trees. The result, published in the journal Nature in May , was unexpected. It seems that each gene studied tells a slightly different evolutionary history.

They concluded that if several genes support a particular architecture, then it must be exact.
