So what is a ‚Äúportfolio‚Äù? This is a collection of assets - usually, but not always - securities. From the point of view of an investor, a portfolio of different securities minimizes the risk of losing all the money invested at once. With relatively common economic assumptions, it can be shown algebraically that each of two stocks with a dispersion of similar size will have a variance greater than a portfolio of the same stocks.

In the broadest sense of the word, an investment portfolio may involve very different assets, far beyond just the shares traded on the stock exchange. For example - debt bonds, various derivatives of stocks and bonds, and so-called ‚Äúdepositary receipts‚Äù - global (‚ÄúGDR‚Äù) or - preferred by Russian companies in the past - American (‚ÄúADR‚Äù). The investment portfolio may include and (so far) non-tradable shares of your startup, apartment, money in an account with Sberbank, a football club, a yacht, etc. Earlier in history, some continued this list of captured women; but I am against it, because then it is necessary to add men, raise themes of slavery and serfs, and other things that we forgot about in our habr-civilization.

Many efficient people intuitively manage their time as an investment portfolio. For example, investing in education is also, as a rule, an asset that differentiates my investment portfolio. A company can diversify by investing in equipment and technology, as well as in people, research, culture, etc. In general, I simply urge to consider portfolio investment more broadly than popular wisdom about laying eggs in different baskets.

Another example, not alien to startups, is a (professional) venture capitalist. Far-sighted, he lays his eggs (money) in different baskets in the hope that at least one of them will hatch good luck chicks, one of which will pay for all the other tales with a less happy ending. At the same time, the capitalist saves 25% -40% for the so-called subsequent (‚Äúfollow-on‚Äù) investments in those chicks whom he considers the most likely success at a later stage. But professional history shows that the real bird of luck almost never emerges from a risk-free project. In other words, in order to increase the likelihood of the emergence of only one bird of luck, you must first take very big risks and then minimize them. So, portfolio diversification is one of the ways to minimize investment risks. By the way, by itself, the diversification of investments, for example, in mutants does not guarantee the appearance of a bird of luck. But about this - like some other time.
