Pyramid rule

Your new site has a modern, up-to-date design. Bulletproof layout. Crystal-clear code that can easily hold 50,000 unique characters. It remains only to write clear and precise texts.

This rule will help you. Despite the fact that it is simple, it is very effective.

Steve Krug and Jacob Nielsen teach us: if the user does not quickly understand whether he needs information, he will leave. The text will be read very fluently, literally one or two sentences.

How to make the text for the site? What to put forward, and what to save for later? When I first asked this question, my father, a former TASS journalist, spoke about the rule of the pyramid. His, in his time, taught by colleagues from the BBC (BBC BBC, not ve-we-es, if that).

Rule itself


At the beginning of the article, put the shortest basic information about what is where and when. It should be stated as briefly and clearly as possible.

The further to the end of the text, the information becomes more detailed, more descriptive. If there are subjective opinions - they should also be left for later.

So, when moving from the beginning to the end of the text, the nature of the information changes:
- from objective to subjective,
- from brief to detailed,
- from clear to diverse.

Historicism of the rules of the pyramid


As I already wrote, my father worked in TASS - in a telegraph agency. From the teletype there was a long tape. An automated typewriter (the concept of "Printer" was not yet there) continuously produced new articles. The teletype attendant had to quickly read and solve everything:
- what to send urgently to the press, as the most important news,
- what to post in the sections "Sport", "Economy", "Hostess on a note" or others,
- what to disregard.

Naturally, the duty officer was tired, and could miss the necessary information. The rule of the pyramid was invented specifically for the fact that on duty teletype could quickly navigate what text they are offered. The conditions were as follows:

- The first paragraph consists of two sentences, a maximum of three, very short, which would immediately give an idea of ‚Äã‚Äãwhat, where, when and in which category it belongs.
- The second paragraph explains the first, it gives the main details of the article.
- Further, more and more detail, more subjectively.

Right or inverted pyramid?


A couple of times I had to hear different versions of how the pyramid is ‚Äúspace-oriented‚Äù.

One version is that the pyramid is turned upside down, stands on the tip: at the top (the widest part) the most important information, the lower, the more minor things are published there.

The second time: the pyramid is normal, expanding to the base. Upstairs everything is very short and concise, the bottom is more and more detailed.

Both versions have the right to exist. The main thing: from the beginning to the end of the nature of the text is changing.
