Introduction


In this article, we will look at the original SAP plan with HANA, and then discuss the recent unpublished change in policy for Oracle, which says a lot about what SAP has internally realized about HANA and its future.

Initial Plan for HANA


The initial SAP plan for HANA was extremely optimistic. He was defended by Hasso Plattner, and the plan was so extensive that a description of all this would be a separate article. However, the following were included:

  • Books: Four books written by Hasso Plattner in the field of working with memory.
  • Marketing and PR: Mass marketing and PR for HANA, which must have been one of the biggest efforts and costs in the history of enterprise software.
  • Exaggerated Benefits: Offers tremendous benefits to HANA, some of which are devoted to whole articles, when articles exaggerate the benefits of HANA .
  • The illusory concept of simplification: the message that because of the simplified model, HANA will actually significantly reduce the complexity of SAP, as well as business processes in projects.
  • Creating an artificial S / 4HANA restriction for HANA: here, SAP took its best-selling ERP system, and in the new version, called S / 4HANA, limited it to HANA. This has always been an anti-competitive step, as there was no true technical reason for creating this restriction. But, as usual, the Federal Trade Commission, as well as the European Trade Commission, did not pay attention to this and did not make a statement. The concept was that many customers would migrate to S / 4HANA, and then they would have to buy HANA. But this approach has not yet been developed, as the market acceptance of S / 4HANA was incredibly low, which is discussed in the article ‚ÄúHow SAP manages the perception of the number of customers‚Äù .
  • Penetration and distribution: SAP adopted the following strategy after HANA was acquired in the database layer. SAP has tools for working with all kinds of databases and products that are provided to customers after they have acquired HANA. One of the problems with HANA is that SAP does not disclose to the customer all the components necessary to launch HANA when he buys a copy of HANA. The customer learns about additional products after they have made a purchase. Secondly, SAP has positioned lagging databases, such as SAP IQ, formally Sybase IQ for archiving data for HANA. The argument was that SAP IQ was much better exchanged with HANA, and this, according to plan, would force companies to crowd out third-party databases, which in fact were much better archived, in favor of SQP IQ.

As Oracle penetrated the application layer using its market share in the database, SAP in turn planned to grow (and not acquire) at the database level. Thus, they intended to oust Oracle from "their" accounts. You should be aware that being a third-party software provider, when you sell software to an SAP account, it is considered that you also own this account.

The SAP transition to the database level was based on inaccurate information. Oracle has penetrated the application layer. This is what I am not a fan of for several reasons: because acquisitions lead to concentration of markets, and concentration of markets invariably lead to less competition and blockage. But Oracle did have real applications that offered real value.

Market inefficiency Acquisitions are usually made by companies that dominate the same space. Oracle in databases. SAP in the field of ERP for large companies. Microsoft in operating systems and office suite. These ‚Äúexcess‚Äù profits are then often used to buy companies in related areas. However, this leads to lower profits. More than half of Oracle‚Äôs profits still come from databases. Three quarters of Microsoft‚Äôs profits come from Windows and Office (and not from XBox, phones, etc.). Acquisitions (and even growth in non-core areas) mean a decrease in the efficiency of the enterprise, as the bureaucracy increases, and the additions are less effective than the core. The only company that seems to oppose this rule is Amazon. It seems that Amazon can move in all directions, and be very effective in doing so. They even migrate to an area like AWS, which has a higher profit than the area in which they started (online retail).

Market Concentration: Acquisitions lead to market concentration, and market concentrations invariably lead to less competition and blockage. This lesson should be reviewed by each generation. But Oracle did have real applications that offered real value. Therefore, although I disagree with this, the Federal Trade Commission (which seems to spend most of its time donating donuts) had to approve the massive acquisition of Oracle. This is why purchasing software in most cases is not a good idea for government approval.

‚ÄúThere was no innovation in SAP outside of HANA and S / 4HANA.‚Äù
