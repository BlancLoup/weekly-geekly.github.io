Cataloging and reserving personal photo archives


In our age of publicly available mirror and mirrorless cameras with a matrix resolution of 16 megapixels and higher, a home photo archive can easily become the place where, as they say, the devil breaks his leg. Due to the size of the files stored in it, the photo archive can easily clog not only a laptop disk, but also a medium-sized NAS or a multi-disk external drive. This passes under the category of "store forever."

Files multiply, clog screw by screw, are lost in the abyss of time and the same names, until in the end the data remains merged into a uniform common mess, in which you can not find anything sensible, and besides, half of the necessary files are worn or defective.

Therefore, cataloging a personal photo library is necessary, as is its regular backup (backup). There are a number of utilities that offer this feature, usually with photo viewing features. However, for most users, such utilities are not very much in demand. In fact, there are two circles of photographers who would fully have enough functionality offered by iPhoto, Aperture or Picasa; these are either amateurs who shoot in JPEG and don‚Äôt bother with the processing, or professionals with a single camera / system purchased for centuries, in which the process of working with photo files is pre-verified and thought through to the last movement. The rest are faced with problems, the range of which I will try to briefly outline below.

  • A lot of files with pictures. One shooting day is easily able to bring a photographer from 500 to 2000 shots. Naturally, some of them will be immediately eliminated, but everything else needs to be processed, and the processed one should be safely stored!
  • Duplicate file names. You will definitely have more than once a situation when you copy a directory with the name ‚Äú100xxxxx‚Äù, the files in which start with ‚ÄúDSCN0001‚Äù into a folder where there is already such a directory, and such file names.   • Different processing algorithms for multiple cameras. In many cases, you have to look for the optimal converter for RAW files shot by a particular camera, since using the same program is not justified by the quality of the output data. As an example, you can use Adobe Lightroom, which, according to many, is insufficiently revealing the color possibilities and the sharpness of a number of modern models. In such cases, alternative developers are used, including native software cameras, RPP , Raw Therapee or Capture One. This eliminates or makes it difficult to use a standard single developer as a cataloger or software for making backups.
  • Storage errors. With a typical intensity of data flow associated with the processing of a variety of photos, the wear and tear of working computer equipment increases, up to the breakdown of working disks. The above problems, as already noted, are solved using two template approaches:

  • Proper organization of photo archives contributes to the preservation, retrieval and use of data in the required mode.
  • Backup (backup) and archiving eliminates the loss of captured and processed photos.

Next, I will briefly describe my personal experience in organizing an amateur photo archive and its supporting infrastructure.

Organization of photo archives working catalogs


No backup utilities will save the situation if there is trash and confusion in the archive itself. Therefore, the first action necessary to preserve the photo archive in order is correct rubrication and cataloging.

Backup, archiving and data security planning


First, I‚Äôll clarify in brief the difference between backup and archiving. Archiving is storing data in a safe place. But backup is storing data in a safe place with regular updates and the ability to quickly recover.
