Heinrich Hertz conducted the first experiments with wireless transmission of information. His experience was a success - he was able to convey a message within one attic of his own house. Actually, this would be the case if the Italian Marconi had not read this remarkable fact in Hertz‚Äôs biography. At that time, an electronic telegraph already existed and, according to Marconi, its device would complement the telegraph where there are no wires. However, Marconi's invention was used for communication on warships, and sending messages to a large number of listeners at the same time remained in the future. Marconi himself did not believe in the brilliant future of radio communications.

By the way, about the ships, more precisely, about the navy - in 1905, in the Tsushima battle, the Japanese fleet defeated the Russian squadron partly "thanks to" the radio equipment that Japanese military leaders bought from Marconi. But this was not the last argument in favor of the complete radioification of the military and civilian fleet. The last word turned out to be another, this time a civil one, a tragedy - the death of the Titanic. After the emergency signals from the sinking giant saved 711 passengers, the maritime authorities of the developed countries of the world ordered that there was radio communication on every sea and oceanic ship, and a special person ‚Äî a radio operator ‚Äî listened to the incoming signals around the clock. But numerous radio amateurs believed. For the First World War, so many amateur radio stations were created that the governments of the countries were in a panic: amateurs connected to military communication sources and listened to the channels. Therefore, the radio became the object of regulation, and there were no longer those who underestimated it. It became obvious that in the hands of mankind a powerful cultural phenomenon, information weapons and promising technology. Although, we are ready to argue, then no one had any idea about the true prospects of radio.

Nevertheless, the radio split the life of mankind in the twentieth century into four parts along three milestones:

  1. November 2, 1920 - the first US commercial radio station, KDKA, went on the air in Pittsburgh
  2. July 1, 1941 - the first commercial television station began broadcasting.
  3. April 3, 1973 - For the first time in history, Martin Cooper from Motorola made a cell phone call.

As you see, both the state and the business understood that radio is information, money, power.
