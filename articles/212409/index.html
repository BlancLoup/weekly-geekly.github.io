A world without accidents



Ann Arbor, Michigan.
‚ÄúHere the road bends. You, most likely, would not pay attention to it, but two years ago a man was shot down here and you could not find the offender, ‚Äùsays Debra Bezzina. But Bezzina told how one man, driving here at high speed, rolled off the road. And he was not the first.

I prepared for the turn. Something unusual happened: a dashboard alarm sounded, a light signal blinked in the corner of the rear-view mirror. Also on a special display, a right turn sign was shown on a blue background, which turned red, warning of danger. Byrd reduces speed.

What just happened was both simple and deep in meaning. This is what, according to Bezzina and Byrd, employees of the University of Michigan Transportation Research Institute (UMTRI), can change the way American drivers are used to getting to their destination. The device in the car caught the signal, assessed the risks and warned us about the danger.

UMTRI van is not the only car here that has such futuristic equipment. I got to Ann Arbor in the last weeks of the 18-month large-scale test of the communication technology between cars. The test was funded mainly by the US Department of Transportation, which wants to find out what safety benefits such devices can provide. A total of 2,800 cars in Ann Arbor are participating in the program called Safety Pilot. All of them are equipped with devices for transmitting indicators of their speed and position to the same machines. And 400 of them also carry an onboard system, which I observed in a UMTRI van plus cameras, to track the driver‚Äôs response to system warnings. Cars, trucks, tractor trailers, and even a bicycle are tied into the system.



‚ÄúThis is the largest deployment of this technology in the world at the moment,‚Äù said Scott Belcher, head of the Intelligent Transport Community of America, a trade association of which the UMTRI is a part.

Steep corners warnings are not the only type of alerts that drivers experienced in Ann Arbor - they were a whole set along with the connection between car pairs and transmitters along the roads. In various difficult situations, such as difficult left turns, approaching trains at level crossings, maneuvering in ‚Äúblind zones‚Äù, abrupt stops in front of cars. With the UMTRI technology used in the van, all this made driving in such situations much safer.

Experts talk about improving these technologies in the future and developing new ones so that each car can share information about its location and speed.

Some futurists talk about the onset of this transport utopia. In the new reality it will be possible to avoid any accidents at all.
